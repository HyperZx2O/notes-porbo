1 Conquest of Makkah (8 AH) ▾
Answer

The Conquest of Makkah took place in 8 AH / 630 CE. It was not a revenge campaign; it was the peaceful restoration of the Kaabah to Tawhid after Quraysh broke the Treaty of Hudaybiyyah. The casus belli was that Quraysh supported Banu Bakr in attacking Khuzaah, who were allies of the Muslims. This treaty violation gave the Prophet Muhammad (PBUH) a legitimate political and moral reason to march toward Makkah.

The Prophet (PBUH) advanced with about 10,000 Muslims. Abu Sufyan, one of the leading opponents of Islam, surrendered at the gates of Makkah and accepted Islam. Instead of humiliating or killing him, the Prophet (PBUH) forgave him. This shows that Prophetic leadership was based on mercy, not personal revenge.

After entering Makkah, the Prophet (PBUH) removed the 360 idols from the Kaabah. Bilal (RA), a former enslaved African Muslim who had once been tortured for Tawhid, gave the Adhan from the top of the Kaabah. This act symbolised the victory of monotheism over idolatry and the victory of equality over racial and tribal arrogance.

The Prophet (PBUH) declared a general amnesty to the people of Makkah: "Go, you are all free." Quran 17:81 summarises the meaning of this moment: "Truth has come and falsehood has vanished; surely falsehood is bound to vanish." Therefore, the conquest was a moral and spiritual restoration, not merely a military victory.

Exam citations / source support: Quran 17:81; Sahih al-Bukhari, Kitab al-Maghazi #4280; Ibn Hisham, Al-Sirah al-Nabawiyyah; Al-Mubarakpuri, Ar-Rahiq al-Makhtum.

8 AH / 630 CE Quran 17:81 10,000 troops General Amnesty

3 Hart's Reformer–Statesman–Economist ▾
Answer

Michael H. Hart ranked Prophet Muhammad (PBUH) first in The 100 because he was uniquely successful on both religious and secular levels. The lecture explains this through three roles: Reformer, Statesman and Economist.

As a Reformer, he abolished female infanticide, tribal arrogance, blood feuds and idolatry. He replaced tribal superiority with the principle that all human beings are equal before Allah. He also reformed moral life by gradually eliminating alcohol, gambling and exploitation.

As a Statesman, he united the Aws and Khazraj through the Madinah Constitution and created a pluralistic city-state based on mutual defence and religious freedom. He also used diplomacy through the Treaty of Hudaybiyyah and letters to rulers, and he forgave enemies at Makkah instead of taking revenge.

As an Economist, he banned riba (usury), established Zakah as a compulsory wealth redistribution system, and created a social welfare structure through Sadaqah and Zakat al-Fitr. These reforms protected the poor and restricted exploitative capitalism long before modern welfare systems.

Therefore, Hart's judgement is supported by history: the Prophet (PBUH) did not only preach a religion; he transformed society, politics and economics. The lecture also quotes Will Durant, who described him as one of the greatest leaders of all time, and Gandhi, who argued that Islam spread through the Prophet's moral power rather than the sword.

Exam citations / source support: Michael H. Hart, The 100 (1978); Will Durant, The Story of Civilization; Mahatma Gandhi, Young India (1924); Hitti, History of the Arabs; Ramadan, In the Footsteps of the Prophet.

4 Buddhism & the Path to Nirvana ▾
Answer

Buddhism is described as non-theistic because it does not centre its worldview on belief in a Creator God. Its main concern is the problem of suffering and the path to liberation from suffering. This separates it from Islam, Christianity, Judaism and Hinduism, where belief in God or gods plays a central role.

The Buddha was Siddhartha Gautama, a prince who renounced luxury after seeing suffering, old age, sickness and death. He did not claim to be God or a prophet. He claimed to be the "Awakened One" who discovered the path to liberation through enlightenment.

The Buddhist path is based on the Four Noble Truths: life involves suffering, suffering is caused by craving, suffering can end, and the way to end suffering is the Eightfold Path (right view, right intention, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness and right concentration).

Islamic salvation is different. In Islam, the goal is Jannah, gained through faith in Allah, righteous deeds, repentance and Allah's mercy. Quran 2:286 teaches that Allah does not burden any soul beyond its capacity. Sahih al-Bukhari #6463 teaches that no one enters Paradise by deeds alone; Allah's mercy is essential. Thus Buddhism emphasises self-discipline toward Nirvana, while Islam emphasises submission to Allah, moral accountability and divine mercy.

Exam citations / source support: Quran 2:286; Sahih al-Bukhari #6463; Harvey, An Introduction to Buddhism; Smith, The World's Religions.

Non-theistic 4 Noble Truths 8-fold Path Quran 2:286 Bukhari #6463
5 Treaty of Hudaybiyyah (6 AH) ▾
Answer

The Treaty of Hudaybiyyah took place in 6 AH / 628 CE. Although many Muslims initially felt its terms were humiliating, Allah described it in Quran 48:1 as "a clear victory." Its importance lies in the fact that it transformed the conflict from war into diplomacy and gave Islam space to grow.

The first stage was the mission: about 1,400 Muslims set out peacefully for Umrah, but Quraysh blocked them at Hudaybiyyah. The second stage was Bayat al-Ridwan: when a rumour spread that Uthman (RA) had been killed, the Muslims pledged loyalty under the tree. The third stage was the treaty: a ten-year truce was agreed and Quraysh indirectly recognised the Muslim state. The fourth stage was the divine verdict: Quran 48:1 declared the treaty a clear victory.

Three unfavourable terms were: Muslims had to return without performing Umrah that year; they could return the next year for only three days; and any Makkan Muslim fleeing to Madinah would be returned to Quraysh, but a Muslim leaving Madinah for Quraysh would not be returned.

The strategic gains were enormous. Islam spread more during two years of peace than during eighteen years of conflict. The Muslim population grew rapidly, leading to the 10,000-strong force at the Conquest of Makkah. Quraysh's later treaty violation also gave the Prophet (PBUH) the legal ground to liberate Makkah. Therefore, what seemed like a setback became a diplomatic triumph.

Exam citations / source support: Quran 48:1; Al-Mubarakpuri, Ar-Rahiq al-Makhtum; Ibn Hisham, Al-Sirah al-Nabawiyyah, Vol. 2.

6 AH / 628 CE Quran 48:1 Bay'at ar-Ridwan 1,400 Muslims
6 Tasawwuf & Ihsan ▾
Answer

Tasawwuf is Islamic spirituality. It focuses on tazkiyat al-nafs, the purification of the soul, and on sincerity in worship. Its purpose is not to replace Fiqh but to give inner life to outward religious practice. It trains the heart to avoid arrogance, envy, hypocrisy, pride and riya.

Its Quranic basis is found in Quran 91:9-10: successful is the one who purifies the soul, and failed is the one who corrupts it. The Hadith of Jibril defines Ihsan as worshipping Allah as if you see Him; and if you do not see Him, then know that He sees you. This is reported in Sahih Muslim, Book 1, Hadith 1.

The most prominent classical scholar in the lecture is Imam Abu Hamid al-Ghazali (d. 1111 CE). His foundational work is Ihya Ulum al-Din, which integrates law, theology, ethics and spirituality.

A scholar who practises Fiqh without Tasawwuf may produce actions that are legally correct but spiritually empty. For example, a prayer may be outwardly valid but inwardly damaged by pride or heedlessness. Therefore, Fiqh disciplines the action, while Tasawwuf purifies the intention.

Exam citations / source support: Quran 91:9-10; Sahih Muslim, Book 1, Hadith 1 (Hadith of Jibril); Al-Ghazali, Ihya Ulum al-Din.

Tazkiyat al-nafs Ihsan Imam Al-Ghazali Ihya Ulum al-Din
7 Life After Death Compared ▾
Answer

Islamic life after death is a definite sequence, not a vague hope. First comes death and entry into Barzakh, the intermediate state until resurrection. Then comes Yawm al-Qiyamah, when all human beings are resurrected and gathered before Allah. After this, deeds are weighed on the Mizan, the scale of justice. People then face the Sirat, the bridge over Hell. The final destination is either Jannah, for those saved by Allah's mercy through faith and righteousness, or Jahannam, for disbelief and evil.

Quran 3:185 states that every soul shall taste death and that final recompense will be given on the Day of Resurrection. Quran 99:7-8 states that whoever does an atom's weight of good will see it, and whoever does an atom's weight of evil will see it. These verses show that Islam teaches personal accountability, divine justice and final judgment.

Comparison of life after death:

HinduismAtman is reborn through reincarnation according to karma.No single final Day of Judgment like Islam; karma determines rebirth.Moksha: liberation from samsara and union/realisation with Brahman.

8 Usul al-Fiqh & the Four Schools ▾
Answer

Fiqh means Islamic jurisprudence. It is the science of deriving practical legal rulings from the Quran and Sunnah through disciplined methodology. It governs worship, transactions, family law, social conduct and public life.

The four primary sources of Usul al-Fiqh are: Quran, the direct word of Allah and the highest source of law; Sunnah, the sayings, actions and approvals of the Prophet (PBUH); Ijma, the consensus of qualified Muslim scholars; and Qiyas, analogical reasoning used when a new case shares an effective legal cause with an established ruling.

Four Sunni schools of Fiqh:

SchoolFounderMajor regionsDistinctive principle
HanafiImam Abu Hanifah (699-767 CE)Iraq, Turkey, South Asia, Central AsiaBroader use of reasoned opinion (ray) and qiyas when texts are not explicit.
MalikiImam Malik ibn Anas (711-795 CE)North Africa, West Africa, parts of ArabiaGives weight to the practice of the people of Madinah and public welfare.
ShafiiImam Muhammad ibn Idris al-Shafii (767-820 CE)Egypt, East Africa, Southeast AsiaSystematised Usul al-Fiqh in Al-Risalah; strong textual method.
HanbaliImam Ahmad ibn Hanbal (780-855 CE)Saudi Arabia and Gulf regionsStrict reliance on Quran and authentic Hadith; cautious use of qiyas.

9 Muakhaat: Ansar & Muhajirun ▾
Answer

Muakhaat was the brotherhood established by the Prophet (PBUH) between the Muhajirun, who migrated from Makkah, and the Ansar, the Muslims of Madinah. It transformed Madinah from a society of tribal rivalry into a society based on faith, mutual responsibility and shared destiny.

The five types of solidarity were: paired fellowship, where each Ansari was paired with a Muhajir; faith over blood, because the bond was based on Islam rather than tribe; willing generosity, as Ansar shared homes, land and livelihood; solidarity in happiness and sorrow, producing social cohesion; and pluralistic extension through the Constitution of Madinah, where different communities lived under a common civic order.

Muakhaat was a prerequisite for the Constitution of Madinah because a written constitution cannot succeed if society remains divided by tribal hatred. It was also a prerequisite for the Islamic economic system because the Muhajirun had left their property in Makkah, and the Ansar's support prevented poverty, resentment and economic collapse. Thus Muakhaat created the social trust necessary for governance, defence and public finance.

Exam citations / source support: Ibn Ishaq, Sirat Rasul Allah; Ibn Hisham, Al-Sirah al-Nabawiyyah; Watt, Muhammad at Medina; Hamidullah, The First Written Constitution in the World.

Muakhaat Ansar & Muhajirun Constitution of Madinah Hijrah

12 Judaism & Islamic Monotheism ▾
Answer

Judaism is the oldest Abrahamic faith studied in the lecture and is closest to Islam in strict monotheism. The Jewish concept of God is expressed in the Shema: "Hear, O Israel: the LORD our God, the LORD is One." This resembles Islamic Tawhid in affirming the oneness of God and rejecting polytheism.

The Torah is the Five Books of Moses and is regarded in Judaism as primary revelation. The Tanakh includes Torah, Prophets and Writings. The Talmud contains the Mishnah and Gemara and functions as a major source of rabbinic law, interpretation and legal reasoning.

Three important ethical principles are Tikkun Olam, meaning repairing the world through justice; Tzedakah, charity as a religious duty rather than optional generosity; and prohibition of Lashon Hara, avoiding harmful or slanderous speech.

The Islamic position is balanced. Islam honours Musa (AS), Ibrahim (AS) and the original Tawrah as divine revelation. Quran 5:44 says the Torah contained guidance and light. Quran 2:87 mentions that Allah gave Musa the Scripture and sent messengers after him. However, Islam also teaches that the Quran is the final preserved revelation and that earlier communities altered or lost parts of revelation over time.

Exam citations / source support: Quran 5:44; Quran 2:87; Quran 3:3; Solomon, Judaism: A Very Short Introduction; Smith, The World's Religions.

Shema 613 Mitzvot Quran 5:44 Quran 2:87

14 Arabic as the Sacred Language ▾
Answer

Arabic is considered the sacred language of Islam because the Quran was revealed in Arabic. Quran 12:2 says that Allah sent it down as an Arabic Quran so that people may understand. Since the Quran, Hadith, Tafsir, Fiqh and classical scholarship were primarily preserved in Arabic, mastery of Arabic is essential for direct engagement with Islamic primary sources.

Without Arabic, a student depends completely on translation. Translation may communicate general meaning, but it cannot fully carry Quranic rhetoric, grammar, legal precision or linguistic miracle. Therefore, Arabic became the universal language of Islamic scholarship from Spain to India.

Islamic civilisation did not destroy other languages; it enriched them. Persian adopted Arabic vocabulary and became a major language of Islamic literature and poetry. Urdu developed through Arabic, Persian and local Indian languages. Ottoman Turkish used Arabic and Persian vocabulary in administration and scholarship. Swahili absorbed Arabic vocabulary through Muslim trade networks on the East African coast. Thus Arabic became a vehicle for intellectual unity while allowing cultural diversity.

15 Halal & Haram Food System ▾
Answer

The Halal and Haram food system is rooted in divine legislation, not cultural preference. Quran 2:168 commands people to eat from what is lawful and good on earth. This means food must be both legally permissible (halal) and wholesome (tayyib). Quran 5:3 lists several prohibited foods and shows that food law is part of obedience to Allah.

Food is Halal when the item itself is permitted, it is acquired lawfully, it is prepared without contamination by Haram substances, and animals requiring slaughter are slaughtered properly in the name of Allah. Halal therefore reflects purity, gratitude, discipline and accountability.

Four major categories of Haram food are: carrion or dead animals not properly slaughtered; flowing blood; pork; and animals dedicated to other than Allah or slaughtered improperly. Intoxicants are also forbidden by Quran 5:90, because they damage intellect and moral responsibility.

The modern global Halal industry, valued at over three trillion dollars, shows that these seventh-century divine laws still have economic and civilisational relevance. Halal certification now affects food production, finance, tourism, pharmaceuticals and global trade. It also shows that Islamic law can shape ethical consumption at a worldwide scale.

Exam citations / source support: Quran 2:168; Quran 5:3; Quran 5:90; Sahih Muslim #2059; Al-Qaradawi, Islamic Culture Between Authenticity and Renewal.

Quran 2:168 Quran 5:3 Quran 5:90 $3 Trillion Industry

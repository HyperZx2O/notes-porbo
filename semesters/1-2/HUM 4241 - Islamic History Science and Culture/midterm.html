The Conquest of Makkah (8 AH) took place when 10,000 Muslims marched on the city without a single drop of blood being shed. Identify the Casus Belli, describe Abu Sufyan's surrender, explain the significance of Bilal (RA) giving the Adhan atop the Kaabah after 360 idols were removed, and state the declaration of amnesty the Prophet (ﷺ) gave to his former enemies with reference to Quran 17:81.
▾
Answer
Reference: Lecture 05–06.pdf, Slide 9

The Conquest of Makkah in 8 AH (630 CE) was a watershed moment in Islamic history, characterised by a bloodless victory and profound mercy. The Casus Belli (cause for war) was the violation of the Treaty of Hudaybiyyah by the Quraysh, who aided the Banu Bakr tribe in a deadly attack against the Banu Khuzah, who were allied with the Muslims. In response to this breach of treaty, the Prophet Muhammad (ﷺ) marched on Makkah with an army of 10,000, the largest force Madinah had ever mobilised. Upon encountering the Prophet at the city gates, Abu Sufyan, the lifelong enemy of Islam and leader of the Quraysh, surrendered. In a profound display of mercy, the Prophet forgave him on the spot, and Abu Sufyan embraced Islam.

Following the peaceful entry into the city, the Prophet (ﷺ) went directly to the Kaabah and purified it by removing the 360 idols that had been placed inside and around it, fully restoring the monotheistic legacy of Prophet Ibrahim (AS). The significance of Bilal (RA), a former Abyssinian slave who had faced severe persecution in Makkah, giving the Adhan atop the Kaabah cannot be overstated; it symbolised the total triumph of monotheism (Tawhid) over idolatry and the destruction of all racial and social hierarchies in Islam. Finally, instead of seeking revenge for years of persecution, torture, and war, the Prophet (ﷺ) declared a general amnesty to his former enemies.

Ulum al-Quran (Sciences of the Quran) is described as the master discipline from which all other Islamic sciences derive their authority. Name and explain FIVE sub-disciplines of Ulum al-Quran — Wahy, Tafsir, Qiraat, Nasikh wal-Mansukh, and Ijaz al-Quran — and for each one state one classical scholar or foundational text associated with it.
▾
Answer
Reference: Lecture 07–08.pdf, Slide 6

Ulum al-Quran (Sciences of the Quran) serves as the foundational master discipline of Islamic scholarship, providing the methodology to understand, preserve, and apply the divine text. The first sub-discipline is Wahy (Revelation), which studies the nature, timing, and contexts of how Quranic verses were revealed to the Prophet (ﷺ); it includes Asbab al-Nuzul (occasions of revelation), which is critical for correctly applying rulings, as referenced in Quran 17:106. The second is Tafsir (Exegesis), the systematic explanation and interpretation of Quranic meanings using Hadith, linguistics, and juristic principles, with Ibn Kathir's Tafsir al-Quran al-Azim serving as a foundational text in this field.

The third sub-discipline is Qira'at (Recitations), which focuses on the seven or ten canonically authenticated modes of Quranic recitation, each traced by a continuous, mass-transmitted chain (mutawatir) back to the Prophet (ﷺ), with the classical scholar Al-Shatibi authoring the foundational text Hirz al-Amani on the subject. The fourth discipline is Nasikh wal-Mansukh (Abrogation), the science of identifying which Quranic rulings supersede or abrogate earlier ones, essential for deriving correct legal rulings and avoiding misapplication of the law, with Ibn Hazm's Al-Nasikh wal-Mansukh serving as a key classical text. Finally, I'jaz al-Quran refers to the miraculous nature of the Quran — its literary, scientific, and prophetic dimensions that no human can replicate, serving as the eternal proof of its divine origin (Quran 2:23) — with the classical scholar Al-Baqillani authoring the seminal work I'jaz al-Quran on this subject.

Buddhism is described as a non-theistic religion — one of the most distinctive features that separates it from all four other major world religions studied in this course. Explain precisely what non-theistic means, describe who the Buddha was and what he claimed about himself, and compare the Buddhist path to Nirvana through the Four Noble Truths and Eightfold Path with the Islamic path to Jannah through faith, righteous deeds, and Allah's mercy, using Quran 2:286 and Sahih al-Bukhari #6463.
▾
Answer
Reference: Lecture 10.pdf, Slides 6 & 9; Lecture 07–08.pdf, Slide 13

Buddhism is classified as a non-theistic religion because it fundamentally rejects the concept of a supreme Creator God. Instead of focusing on divine worship or seeking salvation through a deity, Buddhism centres on individual liberation, self-discipline, and the understanding that karma and rebirth operate automatically without divine intervention. The religion was founded by Siddhartha Gautama, who became known as the Buddha, the Awakened One. He explicitly claimed to be a human being who had attained full enlightenment under the Bodhi tree, not a divine incarnation or a prophet receiving revelation from God, teaching that his Dharma (teaching) was a guide to end suffering and famously advising his followers to be a lamp unto themselves.

The Buddhist path to Nirvana (liberation from the cycle of rebirth and suffering) is achieved through understanding the Four Noble Truths — that life involves suffering, suffering is caused by craving, it can be ended, and the way to end it is the Eightfold Path of right view, intention, speech, action, livelihood, effort, mindfulness, and concentration. Because Buddhism denies the existence of a permanent soul (Anatta), Nirvana is the cessation of the illusion of self and the blowing out of the flames of craving and delusion. In stark contrast, the Islamic path to Jannah (Paradise) is deeply theistic and relational. Islam teaches that salvation is achieved through a combination of sincere faith (Iman), righteous deeds (Amal Salih), and ultimately, the mercy of Allah, as stated in Quran 2:286, which affirms that Allah does not burden a soul beyond that it can bear, emphasising individual accountability and divine compassion. Furthermore, Sahih al-Bukhari #6463 clarifies that no one enters Paradise by deeds alone, highlighting that while human effort is required, divine grace is the ultimate deciding factor. Unlike the self-achieved, soul-less liberation of Buddhism, Islam promises the eternal, blissful reunion of the purified soul with its Creator in Jannah.

The Treaty of Hudaybiyyah (6 AH) is declared by Allah "a clear victory" (Quran 48:1) despite its apparently humiliating terms. Describe the FOUR stages of the event, identify THREE unfavourable terms, and explain with specific historical evidence what strategic gains Islam achieved in the two years of peace that followed.
▾
Answer
Reference: Lecture 05–06.pdf, Slide 8

The Treaty of Hudaybiyyah in 6 AH (628 CE) was a masterclass in prophetic diplomacy, unfolding in four distinct stages. The first stage, The Mission, began when 1,400 Muslims set out from Madinah peacefully to perform Umrah, only to be blocked by the mobilised forces of the Quraysh at the Hudaybiyyah plain. The second stage, Bay'at ar-Ridwan, occurred when a rumour circulated that the negotiator, Uthman (RA), had been killed by the Quraysh; in response, the Prophet (ﷺ) took a pledge from his companions under a tree that they would stand firm and fight to the end, demonstrating immense resolve. The third stage involved the Terms Agreed, where a ten-year truce was negotiated, the Muslims were to return to Madinah that year without performing Umrah, and the Quraysh implicitly acknowledged the Islamic state by treating with it as a sovereign entity. The fourth stage was the Divine Verdict, where Allah revealed Surah Al-Fath (48:1), declaring the treaty not as a compromise, but as a clear victory.

On the surface, the treaty contained three highly unfavourable terms that frustrated many Muslims: the Muslims had to return home without completing their Umrah that year; they were only permitted to return the following year for a strictly limited duration of three days; and a controversial clause stated that if a Muslim apostate fled to the Quraysh, they would not be returned, but if a Qurayshi fled to the Muslims, they would be sent back. However, the strategic gains achieved during the subsequent two years of peace were monumental. The cessation of hostilities allowed Islam to spread through diplomacy and dialogue rather than conflict; historically, more people embraced Islam in those two years of peace than in the previous eighteen years of persecution and war combined. The Muslim population doubled before the conquest of Makkah, and the treaty ultimately provided the geopolitical stability that allowed the Prophet (ﷺ) to send letters to global empires, setting the stage for the bloodless liberation of Makkah when the Quraysh eventually broke the treaty.

Tasawwuf (Islamic Spirituality) focuses on the purification of the soul and is described in the lecture as the science that ensures sincerity in all acts of worship. Explain what Tasawwuf is, identify its Quranic basis and the definition of Ihsan from the Hadith of Jibril in Sahih Muslim, name its most prominent classical scholar and his foundational work, and explain why a scholar who practises Fiqh without Tasawwuf produces outwardly correct acts that may be spiritually empty.
▾
Answer
Reference: Lecture 07–08.pdf, Slides 10 & 11

Tasawwuf, also known as Islamic Spirituality or Sufism, is the classical Islamic science dedicated to tazkiyat al-nafs (the purification of the soul) and achieving absolute sincerity (Ikhlas) in all acts of worship. Its Quranic basis is rooted in verses that emphasise spiritual purification, such as Quran 91:9–10, which teaches that whoever purifies the soul has succeeded and whoever corrupts it has failed, alongside Quran 2:238. The foundational definition of Tasawwuf is derived from the famous Hadith of Jibril, recorded in Sahih Muslim, where the Prophet (ﷺ) defined Ihsan (spiritual excellence) as worshipping Allah as if one sees Him, and if one does not see Him, knowing that He sees you. This state of God-consciousness (Taqwa) and mindfulness (Muraqabah) is the ultimate goal of the Sufi path.

The most prominent classical scholar of Tasawwuf is Imam Al-Ghazali, whose magnum opus, Ihya' 'Ulum al-Din (The Revival of the Religious Sciences), brilliantly integrates Islamic law (Fiqh) with inner spirituality. Al-Ghazali demonstrated that outward compliance with the law is meaningless without the inner purification of the heart. This interconnectedness is vital because a scholar who practises Fiqh without Tasawwuf may produce acts that are outwardly correct and legally valid, but spiritually empty. Without Tasawwuf, the heart remains vulnerable to inner diseases such as arrogance, envy, and ostentation (Riya). Therefore, while Fiqh ensures that an act is physically and legally performed according to the Sunnah, Tasawwuf ensures that the act is accepted by Allah by purifying the intention and removing spiritual corruption.

The Islamic concept of Life After Death is a precisely defined sequence — not a vague spiritual hope. Describe in order each stage: Barzakh, Yawm al-Qiyamah, the Mizan, the Sirat, and the final destination of Jannah or Jahannam, using Quran 3:185 and Quran 99:7–8. Then compare this with Moksha and Reincarnation in Hinduism and Nirvana and Rebirth in Buddhism, addressing for each tradition what happens at death, whether individual judgment exists, and what the ultimate destination of the soul is.
▾
Answer
Reference: Lecture 10.pdf, Slide 12

In Islam, Life After Death is a precise, divinely ordained sequence beginning with the grave. Upon death, the soul enters Barzakh, an intermediate state of waiting where it experiences a preview of its ultimate fate until the end of the world. This is followed by Yawm al-Qiyamah (the Day of Resurrection), where all of humanity is physically resurrected to face individual accountability for every deed, as emphasised in Quran 3:185, which states that every soul shall taste death and will only receive its full reward on the Day of Resurrection. On this day, the Mizan (the Scale) is established, where a person's good and bad deeds are weighed with absolute justice, reflecting Quran 99:7–8, which teaches that whoever does an atom's weight of good will see it, and whoever does an atom's weight of evil will see it. Following the weighing, the individual must cross the Sirat, a bridge stretched over Hellfire. The final destination is either Jannah (Paradise), a realm of eternal bliss for the believers, or Jahannam (Hell), a place of punishment, from which some monotheists may eventually be extracted after purification.

In stark contrast, Hinduism operates on the concept of Samsara (the cycle of death and rebirth). At death, the eternal soul (Atman) transmigrates into a new physical body based on accumulated Karma. There is no single day of individual judgment by a divine judge; rather, the moral law of Karma operates automatically. The ultimate destination is Moksha, the final liberation from the cycle of reincarnation, where the individual soul merges completely into Brahman (the impersonal, ultimate divine reality). Buddhism similarly believes in rebirth, but fundamentally rejects the existence of a permanent soul (Anatta); at death, it is not a soul that transmigrates, but a karma-energy that flows into a new consciousness. Like Hinduism, there is no divine judgment day, as karma dictates the realm of rebirth. The ultimate destination in Buddhism is Nirvana, or Parinirvana after death, which is not a physical place of bliss, but the complete cessation of the rebirth cycle and the blowing out of all craving, hatred, and delusion.

Fiqh (Islamic Jurisprudence) governs a Muslim's every lawful act through four primary sources known as Usul al-Fiqh. Explain each of the FOUR sources — Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, and Qiyas. Then compare the FOUR Sunni schools — Hanafi, Maliki, Shafii, and Hanbali — by identifying each founder with his dates, the regions where each school is predominant, and ONE distinguishing methodological principle of each school.
▾
Answer
Reference: Lecture 07–08.pdf, Slide 8

Fiqh (Islamic Jurisprudence) is the science of deriving practical legal rulings (ahkam) from primary sources through a systematic methodology known as Usul al-Fiqh. The first and supreme source is the Quran, the literal Word of Allah, which provides the foundational divine legislation for theology, ethics, and law. The second source is the Sunnah, the recorded sayings, actions, and tacit approvals of Prophet Muhammad (ﷺ), which explain, elaborate, and provide practical models for the Quranic rulings. The third source is Ijma (Scholarly Consensus), the unanimous agreement of qualified Islamic jurists of a particular era on a specific legal issue, providing binding authority based on the Prophetic tradition that this Ummah will not agree upon an error. The fourth source is Qiyas (Analogical Reasoning), the process of applying a ruling from the Quran or Sunnah to a new situation because it shares the same effective cause ('illah), allowing Islamic law to remain applicable to novel issues.

From these sources emerged the four major Sunni schools of jurisprudence, each with distinct methodological principles. The Hanafi School was founded by Imam Abu Hanifa (699–767 CE) and is predominant in Iraq, Turkey, South Asia, Central Asia, and Egypt; its distinguishing principle is a heavy emphasis on Ra'y (reasoned opinion) and Qiyas when explicit texts are not decisive, making it highly flexible in commercial and civil law.

The Maliki School was founded by Imam Malik ibn Anas (711–795 CE) and is predominant in North and West Africa, and historically in Spain; it is distinguished by its prioritisation of the practice of the people of Madinah as a living, mass-transmitted Sunnah, alongside a strong emphasis on Maslaha (public welfare) in novel cases.

The Shafi'i School was founded by Imam al-Shafi'i (767–820 CE) and is predominant in Southeast Asia, Egypt, East Africa, and Kurdistan; al-Shafi'i was the first to systematically codify Usul al-Fiqh, and his school is distinguished by a strict prioritisation of Hadith over local custom, balancing the Hanafi reliance on reason with the Maliki reliance on tradition.

The Hanbali School was founded by Imam Ahmad ibn Hanbal (780–855 CE) and is predominant in Saudi Arabia and the Gulf States; it is the most conservative school, distinguished by its strict restriction of Qiyas and juristic opinion, relying almost exclusively on the Quran and authenticated Sunnah while strongly resisting Bid'ah (innovation) in matters of worship.

HanafiMalikiShafi'iHanbali

The Muakhaat (Brotherhood) between the Ansar and the Muhajirun transformed the social fabric of Madinah within months of the Hijrah. Explain what Muakhaat was, describe the FIVE types of solidarity it created, and explain why historians describe it as a prerequisite without which neither the Constitution of Madinah nor the economic system of the Islamic state could have functioned.
▾
Answer
Reference: Lecture 05–06.pdf, Slide 6

The Muakhaat (Brotherhood) was a revolutionary social institution established by the Prophet Muhammad (ﷺ) shortly after his migration to Madinah, wherein he formally paired each Ansar (the local helpers of Madinah) with a Muhajir (the emigrant from Makkah). This was not merely a symbolic gesture, but a binding covenant of shared destiny, where the Ansar willingly shared their wealth, homes, and livelihoods with the displaced Muslims who had left everything behind in Makkah. This act fundamentally restructured the societal paradigm, shifting the basis of human connection from tribal lineage to shared faith.

The Muakhaat created five distinct types of solidarity that cemented the new society. First, Paired Fellowship established a direct, personal bond of mutual support and shared resources between specific individuals. Second, Faith Over Blood revolutionised social bonds by proving that brotherhood was built on shared values and faith rather than ethnicity, tribe, or ancestry. Third, Willing Generosity was demonstrated as the Ansar spontaneously offered portions of their agricultural land and property to their new brothers without coercion. Fourth, Solidarity in All States ensured that the community was deeply connected in both sorrow and happiness, creating profound social cohesion across diverse groups. Fifth, Pluralistic Extension allowed this model of mutual respect and shared destiny to extend to interfaith coexistence, integrating Muslims, Jews, and others into a unified city-state under the Charter.

Historians describe the Muakhaat as an absolute prerequisite for the success of the Islamic state because it solved the immediate crisis of mass displacement and poverty among the Muhajirun. Without this economic safety net and social integration, the newly formed state would have collapsed under internal refugee crises and tribal resentment. By forging an unbreakable social fabric, the Muakhaat provided the necessary trust, unity, and economic stability that allowed the Constitution of Madinah to function and the broader administrative and economic systems of the Islamic state to flourish.

Islamic art and architecture are described as "the Aesthetic of Tawhid" — every element expressing the Oneness of Allah rather than human greatness. Describe FOUR distinctive features — geometric patterns, Arabic calligraphy, arabesques, and the structural elements of the mosque — explaining the theological meaning behind each. Name THREE iconic monuments from three different continents and state what each demonstrates about Islamic civilisation. Support your answer with Sahih Muslim, Hadith 91.
▾
Answer
Reference: Lecture 09.pdf, Slide 9

Islamic art and architecture are fundamentally rooted in the concept of Tawhid (the Oneness of Allah), serving as a visual manifestation of divine unity, infinity, and order rather than a celebration of human ego. This aesthetic is driven by four distinctive features. First, geometric patterns utilise complex mathematical tessellations and star patterns; theologically, they express the infinite nature of Allah and the underlying cosmic order without resorting to figurative representation. Second, Arabic calligraphy is considered the highest art form in Islam, as it physically renders the Word of Allah (the Quran) beautifully, adorning walls and manuscripts to constantly remind the believer of divine speech. Third, arabesques feature intertwining vines and floral motifs that symbolise the infinite growth and vitality of creation under the sustenance of the One Creator. Fourth, the structural elements of the mosque — such as the dome, minaret, muqarnas (honeycomb vaulting), and the central courtyard — are designed to direct the heart upward toward the heavens and create a unified, egalitarian space for communal worship, devoid of hierarchical seating.

This profound aesthetic is guided by the Prophetic tradition recorded in Sahih Muslim, Hadith 91, where the Prophet (ﷺ) said that Allah is beautiful and loves beauty, elevating artistic excellence to an act of worship. Islamic civilisation's global reach and architectural mastery are demonstrated by three iconic monuments across three different continents: Masjid al-Haram in Makkah (Asia), which demonstrates the spiritual epicentre and universal unity of the global Muslim Ummah during Hajj; the Alhambra Palace in Granada, Spain (Europe), which showcases the zenith of Islamic art, science, and governance in Andalusia through its breathtaking courtyards and intricate stucco work; and the Hassan II Mosque in Casablanca, Morocco (Africa), which demonstrates the enduring integration of traditional Moroccan craftsmanship with modern engineering, standing as a monumental testament to faith on the Atlantic coast.

Aqeedah governs not what a Muslim does but what a Muslim must believe with absolute certainty. State and explain ALL SIX Pillars of Islamic Faith (Arkan al-Iman) with ONE Quranic reference for each pillar. Then explain in one clear paragraph the fundamental difference between Aqeedah and Fiqh, and why a scholar who masters Fiqh but neglects Aqeedah produces legal rulings without theological grounding.
▾
Answer
Reference: Lecture 07–08.pdf, Slides 9 & 11

Aqeedah (Islamic Theology) defines the absolute convictions a Muslim must hold, comprising the Six Pillars of Faith (Arkan al-Iman). The first pillar is Belief in Allah, which affirms absolute Tawhid — that Allah is One, unique, and without partners, as stated in Quran 112:1–4 (Surah Al-Ikhlas). The second is Belief in Angels, real beings created from light who execute Allah's commands without disobedience, referenced in Quran 2:285. The third is Belief in Divine Books, affirming the original revelations of the Torah, Psalms, Gospel, and Quran, with Quran 4:136 commanding belief in what was revealed to Muhammad and previous prophets. The fourth is Belief in Prophets, accepting that 124,000 prophets were sent to all nations with the same core message of monotheism, culminating in Muhammad (ﷺ) as the final prophet (Quran 33:40). The fifth is Belief in the Last Day, affirming the certainty of resurrection, judgment, Paradise, and Hell, as detailed in Quran 99:1–8. The sixth is Belief in Divine Decree (Qadar), which asserts that all events occur by Allah's knowledge and will, while human free will exists within that preordainment, referenced in Quran 54:49.

The fundamental difference between Aqeedah and Fiqh lies in their subject matter and purpose: Aqeedah addresses the unseen convictions of the heart, namely what a Muslim must believe with absolute certainty, whereas Fiqh addresses the physical actions of the body, namely the practical legal rulings governing worship, transactions, and daily life. Islamic sciences operate as a living ecosystem where each branch nourishes the other. Therefore, a scholar who masters Fiqh but neglects Aqeedah produces legal rulings without theological grounding because they understand the mechanical validity of an act but lack the foundational understanding of why the act is mandated by the Divine. Without Aqeedah, Fiqh becomes a mere set of ritualistic laws devoid of spiritual purpose, God-consciousness, and the correct theological framework that gives those laws their ultimate meaning and authority.

Judaism is described in the lecture as the oldest Abrahamic faith and the tradition closest to Islamic monotheism in its concept of God. Explain the Jewish concept of God using the Shema, describe the role of the Torah and Talmud as sacred texts, identify THREE core ethical principles of Judaism — Tikkun Olam, Tzedakah, and the prohibition of Lashon Hara — and explain the Islamic position on Judaism with reference to Quran 5:44 and Quran 2:87.
▾
Answer
Reference: Lecture 07–08.pdf, Slide 12; Lecture 10.pdf, Slides 7 & 11

Judaism is built upon a foundation of strict, indivisible monotheism, best encapsulated by the Shema, the declaration that the LORD is the God of Israel and the LORD is One. This declaration rejects any concept of Trinity or incarnation, viewing God (YHWH) as transcendent, all-powerful, and personal, making it the closest ancient tradition to Islamic Tawhid. The religious life of Judaism is governed by two primary sacred texts: the Torah, which comprises the Five Books of Moses and serves as the primary written divine revelation containing the 613 commandments (Mitzvot), and the Talmud, the comprehensive Oral Law, consisting of the Mishnah and Gemara, which provides extensive rabbinic commentary, legal debates, and practical applications of the Torah's laws.

Ethically, Judaism places a heavy emphasis on justice and communal responsibility through three core principles. Tikkun Olam translates to repairing the world, mandating acts of justice and social action to heal societal brokenness. Tzedakah is the concept of charity, which is not viewed as an optional act of generosity but as a strict religious obligation to ensure equity and support the vulnerable. The prohibition of Lashon Hara (evil speech or gossip) is strictly enforced in Halakha (Jewish law), emphasising the sanctity of human dignity and the severe moral failure of using words to harm others.

From an Islamic perspective, Judaism is deeply respected as the faith of great Prophets like Ibrahim (Abraham) and Musa (Moses). The Islamic position, referenced in Quran 5:44, affirms that Allah sent down the Torah, wherein there was guidance and light, acknowledging its original divine origin. Furthermore, Quran 2:87 highlights the continuity of the Abrahamic message, confirming the miracles given to Jesus and Moses. Islam affirms the core Abrahamic principles of monotheism, covenantal ethics, and divine law shared with Judaism. However, Islamic theology maintains that while the original Torah was divine, parts of it were altered or lost over time, necessitating the revelation of the Quran as the final, preserved criterion (Al-Muhaymin) over all previous scriptures.

The Muslim army grew from 313 soldiers at Badr (2 AH) to 30,000 at Tabuk (9 AH). For any THREE of the five major campaigns — Badr, Uhud, Khandaq, Conquest of Makkah, and Tabuk — describe the force sizes, the key tactical lesson, and the outcome. Then identify the moral discipline code the Prophet (ﷺ) enforced upon every soldier, stating specifically what categories of people and property were absolutely forbidden from harm.
▾
Answer
Reference: Lecture 05–06.pdf, Slide 15

The evolution of the Muslim army under the Prophet Muhammad (ﷺ) is marked by profound tactical lessons and divine support across several major campaigns. At the Battle of Badr (2 AH / 624 CE), a vastly outnumbered Muslim force of just 313 soldiers faced a Quraysh army three times their size. The key tactical lesson was that faith, strategic positioning (securing the water wells), and divine assistance could overcome overwhelming material disadvantages; the outcome was a decisive Muslim victory that established the political authority of Madinah.

At the Battle of Uhud (3 AH / 625 CE), the Muslim force numbered around 700. The critical tactical lesson was the absolute necessity of strict obedience to the commander's orders; a group of archers abandoned their posts to collect spoils, leading to a devastating counter-attack where 70 Muslims were martyred, and the outcome was a painful defeat that served as a permanent lesson in military discipline.

At the Battle of Khandaq / The Trench (5 AH / 627 CE), 3,000 Muslims in Madinah faced a confederacy of 10,000 besieging forces. The tactical lesson was the innovative use of defensive trench warfare, suggested by Salman al-Farsi, to neutralise the enemy's cavalry advantage, and the outcome was a successful repulsion of the alliance through a combination of the trench, severe weather, and divine intervention, securing Madinah's survival.

Despite the growth in military might, the Prophet (ﷺ) enforced a remarkably strict moral discipline code upon every soldier, establishing early rules of engagement that forbade the targeting of non-combatants and the destruction of infrastructure. The moral code absolutely forbade the harming of women, children, the elderly, and monks or religious figures residing in places of worship. Furthermore, soldiers were strictly prohibited from destroying property, specifically cutting down fruit-bearing trees, burning crops, or slaughtering livestock except for food. This ethical framework ensured that Islamic military campaigns remained bound by divine justice and mercy, even in the heat of battle.

Arabic is described in the lecture as the sacred language of Islam and the universal language of Islamic scholarship. Explain why mastery of Arabic is considered essential for engagement with all Islamic primary sources, describe FOUR sciences within the Arabic language — Nahw, Sarf, Balagha, and Adab — and explain how Islamic civilisation used Arabic as a vehicle to elevate Persian, Urdu, Turkish, and Swahili into rich literary languages of Islamic expression.
▾
Answer
Reference: Lecture 09.pdf, Slide 13; Lecture 07–08.pdf, Slide 10

Mastery of the Arabic language is considered an absolute essential for engagement with Islamic primary sources because Arabic is the direct language of the Quran, the literal Word of Allah. The nuances, grammar, and rhetorical depth of the Quran cannot be fully captured in translation; therefore, direct access to divine revelation, Hadith, and classical jurisprudence requires a profound understanding of the language. As the Prophet (ﷺ) stated, one should love Arabic for three reasons: because he himself was Arab, the Quran is in Arabic, and the people of Paradise will speak Arabic. To facilitate this mastery, Islamic scholars developed four intricate sciences within the language: Nahw (grammar), which governs the syntactic structure of sentences; Sarf (morphology), which deals with the internal structure and derivation of words; Balagha (rhetoric), the science of eloquence and linguistic beauty; and Adab (literature), encompassing poetry, prose, and the rich literary heritage of the Arab people.

As Islamic civilisation expanded globally, Arabic served as the universal sacred language of scholarship and the linguistic vehicle that elevated several regional languages into rich literary traditions. Persian (Farsi) absorbed vast amounts of Arabic vocabulary and script, becoming the premier language of Islamic philosophy, Sufi poetry such as that of Rumi, and administration in the East. Urdu emerged in South Asia as a beautiful synthesis of Arabic, Persian, and local South Asian languages, utilising the Arabic script to express profound Islamic theology and poetry, such as the works of Iqbal. Similarly, Turkish and Swahili adopted the Arabic alphabet and integrated extensive Arabic lexicon to become major vehicles of Islamic scholarship, legal discourse, and literature in their respective regions, testifying to Islam's linguistic inclusivity and the unifying power of the Arabic language.

The Halal and Haram food system in Islam is rooted in divine legislation, not cultural preference. Using Quran 2:168 and Quran 5:3, explain the principles that determine whether food is Halal, identify FOUR specific categories of Haram food with their Quranic or Hadith evidence, and explain how the global Halal food industry today — valued at over three trillion dollars — demonstrates the enduring economic and civilisational relevance of these 7th-century divine laws.
▾
Answer
Reference: Lecture 09.pdf, Slide 14

The Islamic dietary law is a comprehensive system rooted in divine legislation, designed to ensure purity, gratitude, and obedience to Allah. The foundational principle determining whether food is Halal (permitted) is outlined in Quran 2:168, which commands mankind to eat of that which is lawful and good on the earth, emphasising that food must be both permissible and wholesome. According to Quran 5:4–5, for food to be Halal, it must meet specific criteria: it must be free from pork and its by-products, contain no intoxicants, and any animal must be slaughtered by a Muslim, Christian, or Jew while invoking the name of Allah (Tasmiyah).

Conversely, Quran 5:3 explicitly outlines categories of Haram (prohibited) food. Four specific categories include pork, explicitly forbidden in Quran 2:173 and 5:3 and considered physically and spiritually impure; blood, consumed as a fluid and forbidden in 5:3; carrion, meaning dead animals that were not properly slaughtered and forbidden in 5:3; and intoxicants such as alcohol and drugs, forbidden in Quran 5:90 because they cloud the intellect. Additionally, any animal slaughtered in the name of other than Allah or carnivorous animals are strictly prohibited.

Today, the global Halal food industry, valued at over three trillion dollars, demonstrates the profound enduring economic and civilisational relevance of these 7th-century divine laws. It is not merely a religious dietary restriction but a massive global economic sector that dictates supply chain management, ethical sourcing, and international trade standards. The Halal industry ensures ethical treatment of animals, prohibits exploitative or harmful consumables, and provides a unified, trustworthy standard for nearly two billion Muslims worldwide. This proves that Islamic divine legislation is not an archaic relic, but a timeless, practical framework that continues to shape global commerce, public health, and civilisational identity in the modern era.

The six Pillars of Islamic Faith and the Five Pillars of Islamic Practice together form the complete framework of a Muslim's life. Explain the FIVE Pillars of Islamic Practice — Shahadah, Salah, Zakah, Sawm, and Hajj — providing for each pillar its definition, its Quranic basis, and one way it contributes to the social cohesion and collective welfare of the Muslim community beyond its individual spiritual dimension.
▾
Answer
Reference: Lecture 07–08.pdf, Slide 15; Lecture 10.pdf, Slide 10

The Five Pillars of Islamic Practice form the physical and structural foundation of a Muslim's life, translating internal faith into outward action. The first pillar is the Shahadah (Declaration of Faith), defined as the sincere testimony that there is no god but Allah, and Muhammad is His messenger. It is the entry point into Islam and the foundation of all practice; beyond its spiritual meaning, it creates profound social cohesion by establishing a universal, egalitarian brotherhood, since anyone, regardless of race or class, who recites the Shahadah is instantly an equal member of the global Muslim community. The second pillar is Salah (five daily prayers), which involves performing ritual prayers at fixed times, as referenced in Quran 29:45. Socially, the congregational prayers, especially the weekly Friday Jumu'ah, serve as a powerful equaliser where kings and commoners stand shoulder-to-shoulder, fostering local community bonds, mutual awareness, and collective discipline.

The third pillar is Zakah (annual obligatory charity), defined as giving 2.5% of one's accumulated savings above a certain threshold to the needy, with its Quranic basis found in numerous verses, such as Quran 9:103. Beyond purifying the individual's wealth, Zakah is a divinely mandated economic tool that prevents the hoarding of wealth, eradicates extreme poverty, and ensures the collective social welfare and financial safety net of the community. The fourth pillar is Sawm (fasting during the month of Ramadan from dawn to sunset), based on Quran 2:183. Socially, the shared experience of hunger and thirst builds deep empathy for the less fortunate, while the communal Iftar meals and nightly Tarawih prayers create the strongest social cohesion and charitable spirit in the Islamic calendar. Finally, the fifth pillar is Hajj (pilgrimage to Makkah once in a lifetime for those able), referenced in Quran 3:97. It contributes to collective welfare by manifesting the ultimate universal brotherhood of Islam, bringing millions of diverse Muslims together in a single location, stripping away all markers of wealth and status, and uniting the global Ummah in a singular, peaceful demonstration of faith and equality.

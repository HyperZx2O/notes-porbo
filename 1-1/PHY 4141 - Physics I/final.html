Chapter One · Electromagnetism

Magnetic Fields

Sources of magnetic fields, force on charges and currents, motion in magnetic fields, Lorentz force applications, and torque on current loops.

D Definitions & Concepts

▼

Sources of Magnetic Fields

  • Moving charged particles (e.g. current in a wire) → electromagnets.
  • Intrinsic spin of elementary particles (e.g. electrons) → permanent magnets.
  • Individual magnetic charges (monopoles) predicted by some theories but never confirmed to exist.

The Magnetic Field B — Definition

Defined via the force it exerts on a moving test charge q with velocity v:

F = qv × B
  • F is perpendicular to both v and B.
  • |F| = qvB sinθ, where θ is the angle between v and B.
  • SI unit of B: Tesla (T) = N/(C·m/s) = N·A⁻¹·m⁻¹. Also: 1 T = 10⁴ gauss.

Magnetic Field Lines

  • Closed loops entering one end (south pole) and exiting the other (north pole).
  • A magnet with two poles is a magnetic dipole.
  • Unlike electric field lines, magnetic field lines never start or end — there are no magnetic monopoles.

The Lorentz Force

A charge q in both an electric field E and magnetic field B experiences both forces:

F = qE + qv × B

When E and B are perpendicular ("crossed fields"), special applications arise: velocity selector, mass spectrometer, Hall effect.

Hall Effect (1879)

When a current-carrying conductor is in a magnetic field B, charges accumulate on its edges, producing a transverse Hall voltage ΔV_H.

  • Sign of ΔV_H → identifies the sign of charge carriers.
  • Magnitude → gives the drift speed v_d.
  • Used to measure magnetic field strength.

Magnetic Dipole Moment

For a current loop of area A carrying current I with N turns:

m = NIA

Unit: A·m². Potential energy in external field: U = −m · B

∂ Derivations

▼
1 · Motion of a Charged Particle in a Uniform Magnetic Field

Case 1 — Velocity perpendicular to B (circular motion):

Magnetic force = centripetal force:

qvB = mv²/r
RADIUS
r = mv / qB
PERIOD
T = 2πm / qB

Cyclotron (angular) frequency — independent of speed:

ω = qB / m

Case 2 — Velocity at angle to B (helical motion):

v⊥ = v sinθ → circular motion at cyclotron frequency
v‖ = v cosθ → uniform linear motion parallel to B
Combined → helix
T and ω are independent of the particle's speed — the basis of the cyclotron particle accelerator.
2 · Applications of Crossed Fields (E ⊥ B)

① Velocity Selector:

Electric force qE (down) balances magnetic force qvB (up):
qE = qvB
v = E / B

② Mass Spectrometer:

After velocity selection, particles enter a second field B₀ and travel a semicircle of radius r:

r = mv / qB₀ = mE / qBB₀
m/q = rBB₀ / E
J. J. Thomson used this method in 1897 to measure the mass-to-charge ratio of electrons.

③ Hall Effect:

Equilibrium condition: qE_H = qv_d B
E_H = v_d B → ΔV_H = v_d Bd
v_d = E_H / B = ΔV_H / (Bd)
3 · Magnetic Force on a Current-Carrying Conductor

For a straight wire segment of length L, cross-section A, carrying current I in field B:

Force on one charge: F = qv_d × B
Total force = (nqAL)(v_d × B) where n = charge density
Since I = nqv_d A:
F = IL × B

For an arbitrarily shaped wire:

F = I ∫ ds × B

Key result: Closed loop in uniform B: net force = 0

4 · Torque on a Rectangular Current Loop — Magnetic Dipole

Loop of sides a × b, N turns, current I, in uniform field B:

Force on each a-side: F = NIaB
Moment arm = (b/2)sinθ each side
τ = NIAB sinθ = m × B

Potential Energy:

U = −m · B = −mB cosθ
This is the principle behind electric motors — torque on a current loop drives rotation against the field.

Chapter Two · Electromagnetism

Magnetic Fields Due to Currents

Biot-Savart Law, Ampère's Law, fields of wires, loops, solenoids, toroids, and infinite sheets.

D Definitions & Concepts

▼

Biot-Savart Law

The magnetic field dB produced at a point P by a current element ids at distance r:

dB = (μ₀/4π) · (I ds × r̂) / r²
  • μ₀ = 4π × 10⁻⁷ T·m/A (permeability of free space)
  • Direction: right-hand rule on ds × r̂
  • Total field: B = (μ₀/4π) ∫ (I ds × r̂) / r²

Ampère's Law

For any closed Amperian loop, the line integral of B around the loop equals μ₀ times the total enclosed current:

∮ B · ds = μ₀ I_enc

Most powerful when the problem has high symmetry (cylindrical, planar, toroidal).

Solenoid

A tightly wound helical coil of wire. For an ideal solenoid:

  • B = 0 outside
  • B is uniform inside, parallel to axis
  • n = N/l = turns per unit length

Toroid

A solenoid bent into a closed doughnut shape. By symmetry B forms concentric circles inside only. B = 0 in the central hole and outside.

Force Between Parallel Wires

  • Parallel currents attract
  • Anti-parallel currents repel
  • Force per unit length: F/L = μ₀I₁I₂ / 2πd
  • Definition of the Ampere: 1 A produces a force of 2 × 10⁻⁷ N/m between infinite parallel wires 1 m apart.

∂ Derivations

▼
1 · Long Straight Wire (Biot-Savart / Ampère)

Using Ampère's law with circular loop of radius r:

∮ B · ds = B(2πr) = μ₀I
B = μ₀I / 2πr   (outside wire, r ≥ R)

Inside the wire (r ≤ R, uniform current density):

I_enc = I₀(r/R)² → B(2πr) = μ₀I₀(r/R)²
B = (μ₀I₀ / 2πR²) r   (inside wire, r ≤ R)
B grows linearly inside the wire, peaks at the surface, then falls as 1/r outside.
2 · Circular Current Loop (Biot-Savart)

For a loop of radius R, axial point P at distance x from centre:

B = μ₀IR² / [2(R² + x²)^(3/2)]
At centre (x=0)
B = μ₀I / 2R
Far field (x≫R)
B ≈ μ₀IR² / 2x³ = μ₀m / 2πx³
(magnetic dipole)
3 · Solenoid (Ampère's Law)

Rectangular Amperian path of length L inside solenoid — only the side parallel to B inside contributes:

∮ B · ds = BL = μ₀(nL)I
B = μ₀nI = μ₀(N/l)I   (inside solenoid)

B is uniform and independent of position inside an ideal solenoid.

4 · Toroid (Ampère's Law)

Circular Amperian path of radius r inside the toroid, enclosing total current NI:

∮ B · ds = B(2πr) = μ₀NI
B = μ₀NI / (2πr)   (a ≤ r ≤ b)
Outside (r > b)
B = 0
Inside hole (r < a)
B = 0
5 · Infinite Conducting Sheet (Ampère's Law)

Sheet in yz-plane, surface current density Js (A/m). Rectangular Amperian path of width w:

∮ B · ds = 2Bw = μ₀Jsw
B = μ₀Js / 2   (uniform, independent of distance)

Chapter Three · Electromagnetism

Induction & Inductance

Faraday's law, Lenz's law, motional EMF, self/mutual inductance, RL circuits, energy in magnetic fields, and 9 worked problems.

D Definitions & Concepts

▼

Magnetic Flux

The flux of B through a surface S:

Φ_B = ∫∫_S B · dA

SI unit: Weber (Wb) = T·m². For uniform B at angle θ: Φ_B = BA cosθ.

Faraday's Law of Electromagnetic Induction

An EMF is induced in a circuit whenever the magnetic flux through it changes:

ε = −dΦ_B / dt
  • Faster flux change → greater induced EMF.
  • The minus sign comes from Lenz's law.
  • For N turns: ε = −N dΦ_B/dt

Lenz's Law

"An induced current has a direction such that its magnetic field opposes the change in flux that caused it."

Self-Inductance

When a circuit's own current changes, the changing flux induces a "back EMF" opposing the change:

ε_L = −L dI/dt

SI unit: Henry (H). L depends only on geometry.

Mutual Inductance

When current i₁ in coil 1 changes, it induces an EMF in coil 2:

ε₂ = −M di₁/dt

M = mutual inductance (H). M₁₂ = M₂₁ = M.

RL Circuit Behaviour

  • Inductor opposes instantaneous changes in current.
  • Current rises exponentially from 0 to I₀ = ε/R.
  • Time constant τ = L/R (time to reach 63.2% of final value).

Ampère-Maxwell Law (Displacement Current)

Maxwell extended Ampère's law to handle time-varying electric fields. Displacement current: I_d = ε₀ dΦ_E/dt

∮ B · ds = μ₀(I_c + I_d)

∂ Key Derivations

▼
1 · Motional EMF — Conducting Bar on Rails

A bar of length L moves at velocity v along rails in uniform B (into page):

Work per unit charge = force per unit charge × L = v_d B L
ε = BLv

If the bar has resistance R:

I = ε/R = BLv/R

Rotating bar: One end fixed, length l, angular velocity ω:

ε = ½Bωl²
2 · Inductance of a Solenoid

For a solenoid with N turns, length l, cross-section A:

Flux linkage = N Φ_B = N B A = N (μ₀nI) A = μ₀N²A I / l
L = μ₀N²A / l = μ₀n²Al
3 · Energy Stored in an Inductor

Work done against back-EMF to build up current from 0 to I:

dU = P dt = ε_L · I dt = L I dI
U = ½LI²

Energy density in magnetic field:

u_B = B²/2μ₀
4 · RL Circuit — Current Growth

With EMF ε, resistance R, inductance L — Kirchhoff's voltage law gives:

ε = IR + L dI/dt → dI/(I₀−I) = (R/L)dt

Integrating from 0 to t:

I(t) = I₀(1 − e^(−t/τ))   where τ = L/R

Modern Physics · Pages 20–26

Quantum Theory of Light · Photoelectric & Compton Effects

Definitions, derivations of Einstein's photoelectric equation and Compton scattering formula, photon momentum, and 10 worked problems.

D Definitions

▼

1. Electromagnetic Wave

A wave consisting of oscillating electric and magnetic fields perpendicular to each other and to the direction of propagation. Travel at speed c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s with no medium required.

2. Photon (Quantum of Light)

A discrete packet (quantum) of electromagnetic energy. Light is made of photons. A photon has no rest mass but carries energy proportional to its frequency.

E = hν  |  h = 6.625 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s (Planck's constant)

3. Photoelectric Effect

The ejection of electrons from a metal surface when light of sufficient frequency shines on it. The emitted electrons are called photoelectrons. Discovered by Hertz; electrons identified by Thomson.

4. Work Function (φ)

The minimum energy required to remove an electron from the surface of a metal. A fixed property of the material.

φ = hν₀  |  where ν₀ is the threshold frequency

5. Threshold Frequency (ν₀)

The minimum frequency of light that can eject electrons from a metal surface. If ν < ν₀, no photoelectrons are emitted regardless of intensity.

ν₀ = φ/h  |  No emission if ν < ν₀

6. Stopping Potential (V₀)

The minimum reverse voltage that reduces photocurrent to zero. Used to measure maximum kinetic energy of emitted electrons.

K_max = eV₀ = ½mv²_max  |  e = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C

7. Compton Effect

The scattering of a photon by a free electron, in which the photon loses energy (wavelength increases) and the electron recoils. Direct proof that photons behave as particles with momentum. Discovered by A.H. Compton.

8. Compton Wavelength

The characteristic length scale appearing in the Compton scattering formula. Represents the maximum wavelength shift per scattering event.

λ_C = h/(m₀c) = 0.0024 nm  (for an electron)

9. Photon Momentum

Although a photon has zero rest mass, it carries momentum — making the Compton effect possible.

p = E/c = hν/c = h/λ

10. Rest Energy (E₀)

The energy an electron possesses by virtue of its mass, appearing in the Compton derivation.

E₀ = m₀c²  |  For electron: m₀c² = 0.511 MeV = 511 keV

∂ Derivations

▼
Derivation 1 — Einstein's Photoelectric Equation
1
Energy conservation. A photon carries energy E = hν. When it hits the metal, all this energy is absorbed by one electron.
2
Escape energy. The electron needs minimum energy φ (work function) just to get out.
3
Conservation of energy:
hν = φ + K_max
∴ K_max = hν − φ  →  ½mv²_max = hν − φ
4
Substitute φ = hν₀:
½mv²_max = h(ν − ν₀)
5
Using stopping potential:
eV₀ = hν − hν₀
½mv²_max = hν − φ  |  eV₀ = hν − hν₀   ← Einstein's Photoelectric Equation
Key insight: K_max depends only on frequency ν, not intensity. Intensity only affects number of photoelectrons.
Derivation 2 — Compton Scattering Formula
1
Setup: Photon of frequency ν hits stationary electron. After: photon scatters at angle φ with frequency ν', electron recoils at angle θ.
2
Energy conservation:
hν + m₀c² = hν' + E_electron  →  T = hν − hν'
3
Momentum conservation (x):
p·cosθ = hν/c − (hν'/c)cosφ  … (i)
4
Momentum conservation (y):
p·sinθ = (hν'/c)sinφ  … (ii)
5
Eliminate θ — square and add (i)+(ii):
p²c² = (hν)² + (hν')² − 2(hν)(hν')cosφ  … (iii)
6
Relativistic energy-momentum for electron:
p²c² = (hν−hν')² + 2m₀c²(hν−hν')  … (iv)
7
Equate (iii)=(iv) and simplify:
2m₀c²(hν − hν') = 2(hν)(hν')(1 − cosφ)
8
Divide and convert to wavelengths:
λ' − λ = (h/m₀c)(1 − cosφ)
Δλ = λ' − λ = (h/m₀c)(1 − cosφ)  ← Compton Scattering Formula
Angle φcosφ1 − cosφΔλ
0°100 nm

Key: Δλ depends only on scattering angle φ, not on original wavelength λ.
Derivation 3 — Photon Momentum from Relativity
1
Relativistic energy-momentum relation:
E² = (m₀c²)² + (pc)²
2
For a photon, m₀ = 0:
E = pc  ⟹  p = E/c
3
Substitute E = hν:
p = hν/c
4
Use c = νλ:
p = h/λ
p = hν/c = h/λ  ← Photon Momentum

Work function of copper = 4.7 eV. Find max velocity of photoelectrons for UV light λ = 100 nm. (hc = 1240 eV·nm; m = 9.1×10⁻³¹ kg)

Step 1: E = hc/λ = 1240/100 = 12.4 eV
K_max = E − φ = 12.4 − 4.7 = 7.7 eV = 1.232 × 10⁻¹⁸ J

What wavelength produces photoelectrons of max velocity v = 10⁶ m/s from iron? (φ = 4.5 eV; m = 9.1×10⁻³¹ kg)

Step 1: K_max = ½mv² = ½×9.1×10⁻³¹×(10⁶)² = 4.55×10⁻¹⁹ J = 2.84 eV
Step 2: hν = K_max + φ = 2.84 + 4.5 = 7.34 eV

X-ray photon changes from ν = 1.5×10¹⁹ Hz to ν' = 1.2×10¹⁹ Hz after collision. How much KE was imparted to the electron?

T = h(ν−ν') = 6.625×10⁻³⁴ × 0.3×10¹⁹ = 1.988×10⁻¹⁵ J ≈ 12.4 keV
T ≈ 1.99 × 10⁻¹⁵ J ≈ 12.4 keV

Δλ = 0.0024×(1−0.6947) = 0.0024×0.3053 = 0.000733 nm
λ' = 0.0558 + 0.000733 ≈ 0.0565 nm
λ' ≈ 0.0565 nm

Calculate the work function of sodium when threshold wavelength λ₀ = 680 nm.

φ = hc/λ₀ = 1240/680 ≈ 1.824 eV
φ ≈ 1.82 eV

Threshold wavelength for a metal λ₀ = 300 nm. Find KE of electron ejected by λ = 120 nm radiation.

Step 1: φ = hc/λ₀ = 1240/300 ≈ 4.13 eV
Step 2: hν = 1240/120 ≈ 10.33 eV

K_max = 6.2 eV

Photoelectric Effect — Full Lecture Notes

Laws, definitions, derivations, applications, and 15 worked problems on the photoelectric effect.

D Definitions

▼

1. Photoelectric Effect

The phenomenon of ejection of electrons from a metal surface when light of suitable frequency falls upon it. The ejected electrons are called photoelectrons. J.J. Thomson confirmed these are identical to ordinary electrons.

2. Threshold Frequency (ν₀)

The minimum frequency of light needed to eject electrons from a given metal. Below this frequency, no photoelectrons are emitted regardless of intensity.

ν₀ = φ / h

3. Threshold / Cut-off Wavelength (λ₀)

The maximum wavelength of incident light that can eject photoelectrons.

λ₀ = c / ν₀ = hc / φ

4. Work Function (φ)

The minimum energy required to eject an electron from a metal surface. Depends on the nature of the material and its surface condition.

5. Stopping Potential / Retarding Potential (V₀)

The minimum retarding potential applied to the anode to stop the most energetic photoelectrons and reduce photocurrent to zero.

eV₀ = K_max = ½mv²_max

6. Saturation Current

The maximum photocurrent obtained when all emitted photoelectrons are collected. Proportional to the intensity of incident light (more photons → more electrons).

7. Photocurrent

The electric current produced due to the photoelectric effect. Depends on the intensity of incident light (not frequency) for frequencies above ν₀.

⚖ Laws of Photoelectric Effect

▼

Five Laws of Photoelectric Emission

Law 1

For a given material, photoelectric emission occurs only if the incident light frequency ν ≥ ν₀ (threshold frequency). Below ν₀, no emission even with very intense light.

Law 2

The photoelectric emission is an instantaneous process. There is no time delay between incidence of light and emission of electrons (≲ 10⁻⁹ s).

Law 3

The maximum kinetic energy of photoelectrons is directly proportional to the frequency of incident light and independent of its intensity.

Law 4

The number of photoelectrons emitted per second (photocurrent) is directly proportional to the intensity of incident light.

Law 5

The maximum kinetic energy of photoelectrons is independent of the intensity of incident light; it depends only on the frequency.

∂ Derivations

▼
Einstein's Photoelectric Equation

By quantum theory, a photon has energy E = hν. When it hits the metal, it transfers all its energy to one electron. The electron uses minimum energy φ to escape and the rest becomes kinetic energy.

1
Energy conservation: hν = φ + K_max
2
Substitute φ = hν₀:
K_max = h(ν − ν₀)
3
In terms of wavelength (use c = νλ):
K_max = hc(1/λ − 1/λ₀)
½mv²_max = hν − φ = h(ν − ν₀)  |  eV₀ = hν − hν₀
Stopping Potential vs. Frequency (Millikan's Relation)

From Einstein's equation: eV₀ = hν − φ

1
Rearrange: V₀ = (h/e)ν − φ/e
2
This is a linear equation: V₀ vs ν is a straight line with slope h/e and y-intercept −φ/e.
V₀ = (h/e)ν − φ/e  ← V₀ vs. ν graph gives slope = h/e, intercept = −φ/e

Chapter One · Optics

Interference of Light

Wave superposition, coherence, Young's experiment, fringe formation, Newton's rings, and related problems.

D Definitions & Concepts

▼

Nature of Light — Historical Theories

  • Corpuscular Theory (Newton, 1704): Light as tiny particles. Explained reflection and refraction only.
  • Wave Theory (Huygens, 17th c.): Light as waves spreading in all directions. Disproved by interference (Young, 1802).
  • Electromagnetic Wave Theory (Maxwell, 1865–73): Light as EM disturbance; c = νλ. Explained reflection, refraction, diffraction, interference, polarization — but not photoelectric effect.
  • Quantum Theory (Planck, 1900): Light as energy packets (photons).
  • Dual Nature (Einstein, 1905): Wave when transmitted; particle when emitted or absorbed.

Interference

Modification of intensity obtained by the superposition of two or more beams of light.

  • Constructive interference: Resultant amplitude/intensity is greater — waves add in phase.
  • Destructive interference: Resultant amplitude is zero — waves are out of phase.

Principle of Superposition

When two or more waves overlap, the resultant displacement at any point is the algebraic sum of the instantaneous displacements each wave would produce independently.

Coherent Sources

Two sources are coherent if they emit waves of same frequency, nearly same amplitude, always in phase, same wavelength. Essential for a stable interference pattern.

Young's Double-Slit Experiment (1802)

Sunlight through pinhole S₀, then through two pinholes S₁ and S₂. Alternating bright (constructive) and dark (destructive) fringes produced on screen.

  • Closer slits → wider fringes
  • Red light → wider fringes than green (longer λ)

Fringe Width (β)

The distance between two consecutive bright (or dark) fringes:

β = λD / d

λ = wavelength, D = screen distance, d = slit separation

Newton's Rings

When a plano-convex lens is placed on a plane glass plate, an air film forms. Monochromatic light produces concentric circular interference fringes. The centre is dark.

∂ Derivations

▼
1 · Resultant Intensity in Interference

Two waves with same amplitude a and phase difference δ:

y₁ = a sin(ωt)
y₂ = a sin(ωt + δ)

Resultant: y = R sin(ωt + θ), where:

R² = 4a² cos²(δ/2)
I = 4I₀ cos²(δ/2)

Path difference Δ = xd/D, Phase difference δ = 2πΔ/λ

CONSTRUCTIVE (bright)
Δ = mλ (m = 0,1,2,…)
x_bright = mλD/d
I = 4I₀ (maximum)
DESTRUCTIVE (dark)
Δ = (m + ½)λ
x_dark = (2m+1)λD/2d
I = 0
2 · Fringe Width β in Young's Experiment

Distance between consecutive bright fringes:

y_m = mλD/d   and   y_{m+1} = (m+1)λD/d
β = y_{m+1} − y_m = λD/d

Both bright and dark fringes have the same width β.

β ∝ λ (red light gives wider fringes) and β ∝ D (wider screen distance → wider fringes) and β ∝ 1/d (closer slits → wider fringes).
3 · Newton's Rings — Radius Formula

Air film thickness at radius r from centre: t = r²/2R (R = radius of curvature of lens).

Condition for dark rings (destructive): 2t = mλ

r_m = √(mλR)   (dark rings)

Condition for bright rings (constructive): 2t = (2m−1)λ/2

r_m = √((2m−1)λR/2)   (bright rings)
The centre is dark because at the point of contact, t = 0, and there is a phase reversal at the lower surface.

Chapter Two · Optics

Diffraction of Light

Single-slit and double-slit Fraunhofer diffraction, intensity patterns, missing orders, and 5 worked problems.

D Definitions & Concepts

▼

Diffraction

The bending of light waves around obstacles or through narrow openings. Becomes significant when the slit width is comparable to the wavelength of light.

  • Fresnel diffraction: Either source or screen (or both) are at finite distance from the slit.
  • Fraunhofer diffraction: Both source and screen are effectively at infinity (parallel rays — achieved with lenses).

Single-Slit Fraunhofer Diffraction

For a slit of width b, the intensity pattern on a distant screen is:

I = I₀ (sinα / α)²   where α = (π b sinθ) / λ
  • Central maximum at α = 0 (θ = 0)
  • Minima at b sinθ = mλ (m = ±1, ±2, …)
  • Secondary maxima at approximately α = ±3π/2, ±5π/2, …

Half-Angular Width of Central Maximum

The central maximum extends from θ = −θ₁ to θ = +θ₁, where:

sinθ₁ = λ/b

Missing Orders in Double-Slit Diffraction

When d/b = m/p (integer ratio), the m-th interference maximum falls at the p-th diffraction minimum and disappears.

d/b = 2 → missing orders: m = 2, 4, 6, …
d/b = 3 → missing orders: m = 3, 6, 9, …

∂ Derivations

▼
1 · Single-Slit Intensity Pattern (Fraunhofer)

Divide the slit of width b into N small segments, each acting as a Huygens source. For a point at angle θ:

Total phase difference: 2α = (2π/λ) b sinθ
so α = (π/λ) b sinθ

Sum of N phasors spaced 2α/N apart (as N→∞):

A = A₀ · sin(α)/α
I = I₀ (sinα / α)²

Minima: sin α = 0, but α ≠ 0:

α = mπ  →  b sinθ = mλ   (m = ±1, ±2, …)

Secondary maxima: tan α = α, approximately at α = ±3π/2, ±5π/2:

I₁ ≈ I₀/22.2   (first secondary max, ~4.5% of central)
2 · Double-Slit Fraunhofer Diffraction

For two slits each of width b, separated centre-to-centre by d = b + c:

β = (π/λ) b sinθ  (single-slit diffraction parameter)
γ = (π/λ) d sinθ  (two-slit interference parameter)
I = 4A₀² (sin²β / β²) cos²γ

Interference Maxima:

d sinθ = mλ   (m = 0, ±1, ±2, …)

Interference Minima:

d sinθ = (m + ½)λ

Diffraction Minima:

b sinθ = pλ   (p = ±1, ±2, …)

Missing Orders: When d/b = m/p (integer), the m-th interference maximum coincides with the p-th diffraction minimum and disappears.

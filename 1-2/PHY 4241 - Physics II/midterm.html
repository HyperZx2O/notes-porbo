Introduction & Units of Measurement

⏱️ ~2 hours total 📖 Sections 1.1–1.13 🎯 Priority: Low–Medium

1.1 Units & Systems of Measurement

⏱️ 25 min

Every quantity in electrical engineering needs a number + a unit. Without the unit, a number is meaningless — "12" could be 12 volts, 12 ohms, or 12 amps. This course uses the SI (International System), built on the MKS (meter-kilogram-second) standard.

QuantitySI UnitSymbol
Lengthmeterm
Masskilogramkg
Timeseconds
CurrentampereA
Voltage (derived)voltV
Resistance (derived)ohmΩ
Power (derived)wattW
Energy (derived)jouleJ
Exam tip: If a question asks you to "state the unit" of a derived quantity, always give both the name (e.g., "volt") and the symbol (V). Some instructors deduct marks for one without the other.

1.2 Powers of Ten

⏱️ 40 min — practice until automatic

Circuit values span enormous ranges (a resistor might be 2 Ω or 2,000,000 Ω), so powers of ten keep numbers manageable. You must be fluent in all four operations:

Multiplication
(10ⁿ)(10ᵐ) = 10⁽ⁿ⁺ᵐ⁾
Division
10ⁿ ÷ 10ᵐ = 10⁽ⁿ⁻ᵐ⁾
Power of a power
(10ⁿ)ᵐ = 10ⁿᵐ
Add/Subtract
A×10ⁿ ± B×10ⁿ = (A±B)×10ⁿ
EXAMPLE 1.1 — Powers of Ten Arithmetic (based on Boylestad Examples 1.4–1.8)

Problem: Simplify the following:

(a) (1000)(10,000)   (b) (0.00001)(100)   (c) 6300 + 75,000

Part (a)
(1000)(10,000) = (10³)(10⁴) = 10⁽³⁺⁴⁾ = 10⁷ = 10,000,000
Part (b)
(0.00001)(100) = (10⁻⁵)(10²) = 10⁽⁻⁵⁺²⁾ = 10⁻³ = 0.001
Part (c) — must match exponents first
6300 + 75,000 = (6.3 × 10³) + (75 × 10³) = (6.3 + 75) × 10³ = 81.3 × 10³ = 81,300
Common mistake: Students try to add/subtract powers of ten without first making the exponents equal. 6.3 × 10³ + 75 × 10³ works because both have 10³ — you cannot add 6.3×10³ + 7.5×10⁴ directly without converting one of them first.

1.3 Notation: Fixed-Point, Scientific & Engineering

⏱️ 25 min

Scientific notation: exactly one nonzero digit before the decimal point (e.g., 5.34 × 10³).
Engineering notation: the exponent is restricted to a multiple of 3 (...,−6, −3, 0, 3, 6, 9,...), and 1–3 digits are allowed before the decimal point (e.g., 53.4 × 10³ or 5.34 × 10⁴ rewritten as 53.4 × 10³). Engineering notation matches the metric prefixes used on every datasheet and multimeter, which is why it's the more practical form for circuits work.

Each step left or right shifts the exponent by a multiple of 3 — moving the decimal point is just sliding along this line.

EXAMPLE 1.2 — Unit Conversion (based on Boylestad Examples 1.14–1.16)

Problem: Convert 20 kHz to megahertz, and convert 0.002 km to millimeters.

Part (a)
20 kHz = 20 × 10³ Hz = 0.02 × 10⁶ Hz = 0.02 MHz
Part (b)
0.002 km = 0.002 × 10³ m = 2 m = 2 × 10³ mm = 2000 mm
0.02 MHz  |  2000 mm

1.4 Significant Figures & Rounding

⏱️ 15 min

Keep the same number of significant figures as the least-precise measurement in a calculation. For exam purposes: round final answers to 3 significant figures unless told otherwise — this matches the textbook's convention throughout.

CH 2

Voltage and Current

⏱️ ~2 hours total 📖 Sections 2.1–2.12 🎯 Priority: Medium

2.1 Atomic Structure & Charge

⏱️ 20 min

Every atom has a nucleus (protons + neutrons) surrounded by orbiting electrons. Current flow is the organized movement of free electrons in the outermost (valence) shell of conductor atoms. The charge on one electron is:

Charge of one electronQe = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C (coulombs)

It takes 6.242 × 10¹⁸ electrons to make up 1 coulomb of charge.

2.2 Voltage (Electromotive Force)

⏱️ 25 min

Voltage is the work required to move a unit charge between two points. Think of it as "electrical pressure" — the push that drives current through a circuit.

Voltage definitionV = W / Q

Where V = voltage (volts), W = work/energy (joules), Q = charge (coulombs). One volt = the potential difference that requires 1 joule of work to move 1 coulomb of charge.

EXAMPLE 2.1 — Voltage from Work and Charge

Problem: Find the voltage if 48 J of energy are required to move 12 C of charge between two points.

V = W/Q = 48 J / 12 C = 4 V
V = 4 V

2.3 Current

⏱️ 25 min

Current is the rate of charge flow past a point in a circuit:

Current definitionI = Q / t

Where I = current (amperes), Q = charge (coulombs), t = time (seconds). One ampere = 1 coulomb of charge passing a point in 1 second.

EXAMPLE 2.2 — Current from Charge and Time

Problem: If 0.16 C of charge passes through a wire in 5 seconds, find the current.

I = Q/t = 0.16 C / 5 s = 0.032 A = 32 mA
I = 32 mA
Conventional vs. electron flow: Electrons physically move from − to +, but by long-standing convention, current is defined as flowing from + to − (the direction positive charge would move). Always use conventional current direction for circuit analysis unless explicitly told otherwise — this is the standard used throughout the rest of the course.

2.4 Voltage Sources

⏱️ 20 min

A voltage source maintains a fixed terminal voltage by converting some form of energy (chemical, mechanical, light, etc.) into electrical energy. Key types:

  • DC voltage source — fixed polarity and magnitude (e.g., battery)
  • Ideal source — internal resistance = 0, terminal voltage never sags under load
  • Real/practical source — has small internal resistance Rint, so terminal voltage drops slightly under heavy load

2.5 Ampere-Hour Rating & Battery Life

⏱️ 20 min

Battery capacity is rated in ampere-hours (Ah) — the current it can supply for a given time before depleting.

Battery life estimateLife (hours) = Ampere-hour rating / Current drawn
EXAMPLE 2.3 — Battery Life

Problem: How long will a 100 Ah battery last if it supplies a constant 4 A load?

Life = 100 Ah / 4 A = 25 hours
25 hours

2.6 Conductors, Insulators & Semiconductors

⏱️ 15 min
Material typeFree electronsExamples
ConductorMany — low resistanceCopper, silver, aluminum, gold
InsulatorVery few — high resistanceRubber, glass, mica, plastic

2.7 Ammeters & Voltmeters

⏱️ 15 min
  • Ammeter measures current — connected in series in the path of current flow (ideally zero resistance so it doesn't disturb the circuit)
  • Voltmeter measures voltage — connected in parallel across the two points of interest (ideally infinite resistance so it draws no current)
Meter placement — ammeter in series, voltmeter in parallel
Exam favorite: "Where is the ammeter connected — series or parallel?" Series, always. Connecting an ammeter in parallel can short the circuit and blow the meter's fuse.

CH 3

Resistance

⏱️ ~2 hours total 📖 Sections 3.1–3.15 🎯 Priority: Medium

3.1–3.3 Resistance of Wires & Wire Tables

⏱️ 30 min

Resistance of a conductor depends on four factors — material, length, cross-sectional area, and temperature:

Resistance of a circular wireR = ρL / A

Where ρ (rho) = resistivity of the material (Ω·CM/ft in the English/circular-mil system, or Ω·m in SI), L = length, A = cross-sectional area. Longer wire → more resistance. Thicker wire → less resistance.

MaterialResistivity ρ (Ω·CM/ft @ 20°C)
Silver9.9
Copper10.37
Gold14.7
Aluminum17.0
Tungsten33.0
Nichrome675
EXAMPLE 3.1 — Wire Resistance

Problem: Find the resistance of 100 ft of copper wire with a diameter such that A = 810.1 CM (cir. mils).

R = ρL/A = (10.37)(100) / 810.1 = 1037 / 810.1 ≈ 1.28 Ω
R ≈ 1.28 Ω

3.4–3.5 Metric Units & Temperature Effects

⏱️ 25 min

In conductors (metals), resistance increases with temperature (more vibration → more electron collisions). In semiconductors, resistance typically decreases with temperature (more free carriers released).

Resistance at a new temperatureR₂ = R₁ × [(T + t₂) / (T + t₁)]

Where T is the inferred absolute zero constant for the material (T = 234.5°C for copper), t₁ and t₂ are old/new temperatures in °C.

EXAMPLE 3.2 — Temperature Effect on Resistance (copper)

Problem: A copper wire has a resistance of 50 Ω at 20°C. Find its resistance at 100°C.

R₂ = 50 × [(234.5+100)/(234.5+20)] = 50 × (334.5/254.5) = 50 × 1.314 ≈ 65.7 Ω
R₂ ≈ 65.7 Ω — resistance increased with temperature, as expected for a conductor

3.6 Superconductors

⏱️ 10 min

Below a critical temperature, certain materials exhibit zero resistance — current flows with no energy loss. Useful for MRI magnets, maglev trains, and power transmission research. Mostly conceptual/definition-based exam content.

3.7–3.8 Types of Resistors & Color Coding ⭐

⏱️ 35 min — high yield, memorize this cold

Fixed resistors use a 4-band color code. Reading from the band closest to an edge:

  1. Band 1 = first digit
  2. Band 2 = second digit
  3. Band 3 = multiplier (power of ten — number of zeros after the first 2 digits)
  4. Band 4 = tolerance (gold = ±5%, silver = ±10%, none = ±20%)
ColorDigit ValueMultiplier

Brown1×10¹
Red2×10²
Orange3×10³
Yellow4×10⁴
Green5×10⁵
Blue6×10⁶
Violet7×10⁷

White9×10⁹
Gold—×0.1 (tolerance ±5%)

Memory trick from the textbook: Colors run dark → light as values run low → high (Black=0 through White=9). Practice saying "red is 2, yellow is 4" out loud repeatedly — repetition is how this sticks.
Reading a 4-band resistor — Brown / Red / Orange / Gold
EXAMPLE 3.3 — Reading a Color Code (Boylestad Example 3.13)

Problem: A resistor has bands: Brown, Red, Orange, Gold. Find its value and tolerance range.

Digits
Brown = 1, Red = 2 → first two digits = "12"
Multiplier
Orange = ×10³ → 12 × 10³ = 12,000 Ω = 12 kΩ
Tolerance
Gold = ±5% → ±5% of 12,000 = ±600 Ω → range: 11,400 Ω to 12,600 Ω
R = 12 kΩ ± 5% (11.4 kΩ – 12.6 kΩ)
Standard EIA values: Commercial resistors only come in specific values per decade (10, 12, 15, 18, 22, 27, 33, 39, 47, 56, 68, 82 for the 5–20% tolerance range), so that the tolerance bands of adjacent standard values just touch — covering the full range without gaps or excessive overlap.

CH 4

Ohm's Law, Power, and Energy

⏱️ ~2.5 hours total 📖 Sections 4.1–4.9 🎯 Priority: High

⭐ This is the most important chapter in the course

Ohm's Law and the power equations form the backbone of every later chapter. Do not move past this chapter until it's automatic.

4.1–4.2 Ohm's Law

⏱️ 30 min

Ohm's Law relates voltage, current, and resistance for a resistive element:

Ohm's Law (3 forms)V = IR    I = V/R    R = V/I

Memorize the "Ohm's Law triangle": cover the quantity you want to find, and the remaining two show you the operation (multiply if side-by-side, divide if stacked).

The Ohm's Law triangle

Cover the unknown — side-by-side means multiply (V = I×R), stacked means divide (I = V/R, R = V/I).

EXAMPLE 4.1 — Basic Ohm's Law

Problem: A 2.2 kΩ resistor is connected across a 12 V source. Find the current.

Example 4.1 — single resistor across a source
I = V/R = 12 V / 2200 Ω = 5.45 × 10⁻³ A = 5.45 mA
I = 5.45 mA

4.3 Plotting Ohm's Law (I-V characteristic)

⏱️ 15 min

For a fixed resistor, the I-V graph is a straight line through the origin — the steeper the line, the smaller the resistance (since I = V/R, slope = 1/R). Devices that don't follow this straight-line behavior (like light bulbs, where resistance increases as the filament heats up) are called nonlinear.

4.4 Power ⭐

⏱️ 35 min — three equivalent formulas, know all three

Power is the rate of doing work (energy per unit time), measured in watts (W). By combining the definition of power with Ohm's Law, we get three interchangeable formulas:

Basic definition
P = VI
Using resistance & current
P = I²R
Using resistance & voltage
P = V²/R

Use whichever form matches the two quantities you already know — this saves a step versus finding the third variable first.

EXAMPLE 4.2 — Power Dissipation (Boylestad Ex. 4.7)

Problem: What is the power dissipated by a 5 Ω resistor if the current is 4 A?

P = I²R = (4 A)² (5 Ω) = (16)(5) = 80 W
P = 80 W
EXAMPLE 4.3 — Light Bulb Rating (Boylestad Ex. 4.8)

Problem: A light bulb draws I = 0.625 A at its rated voltage of 120 V. Find its wattage rating and its resistance under rated conditions.

Power
P = VI = (120 V)(0.625 A) = 75 W
Resistance
R = V/I = 120 V / 0.625 A = 192 Ω
P = 75 W  |  R = 192 Ω (a "75 W bulb")
EXAMPLE 4.4 — Finding Current from Power (Boylestad Ex. 4.9)

Problem: Determine the current through a 5 kΩ resistor when the power dissipated is 20 mW.

I = √(P/R) = √(20×10⁻³ / 5×10³) = √(4×10⁻⁶) = 2×10⁻³ A
I = 2 mA
Power formula derivation worth memorizing: If P = I²R, then I = √(P/R). If P = V²/R, then V = √(PR). These rearranged forms appear often when power is given and you need V or I.

4.5 Energy ⭐

⏱️ 35 min — classic "cost of electricity" exam question

Energy is power used over time. The practical (utility) unit is the kilowatt-hour (kWh), not the joule:

Energy in kilowatt-hoursW (kWh) = P(W) × t(h) / 1000
EXAMPLE 4.5 — Energy Used Over a Year (Boylestad Ex. 4.11)

Problem: How much energy (in kWh) is needed to light a 60 W bulb continuously for 1 year (365 days)?

W = Pt/1000 = (60 W)(24 h/day)(365 days) / 1000 = 525,600 / 1000
W = 525.6 kWh
EXAMPLE 4.6 — Cost of Electricity (Boylestad Ex. 4.13–4.14 style)

Problem: Find the total cost (at 9¢/kWh) of running: a 1200 W toaster for 30 min, six 50 W bulbs for 4 h, a 400 W washing machine for 45 min, and a 4800 W dryer for 20 min.

Convert each to watt-hours
(1200 W)(0.5 h) = 600 Wh
(6)(50 W)(4 h) = 1200 Wh
(400 W)(0.75 h) = 300 Wh
(4800 W)(0.333 h) = 1600 Wh
Total
W = (600+1200+300+1600) Wh / 1000 = 3700/1000 = 3.7 kWh
Cost
Cost = (3.7 kWh)(9¢/kWh) = 33.3¢
Total cost = 33.3¢

4.6 Efficiency

⏱️ 20 min
Efficiencyη (%) = (Pout / Pin) × 100%

For systems in series (cascade), total efficiency is the product of individual efficiencies: ηtotal = η₁ × η₂ × η₃ × ...

EXAMPLE 4.7 — Cascaded Efficiency

Problem: A power supply (η = 85%) feeds a motor (η = 90%). Find overall efficiency.

η_total = 0.85 × 0.90 = 0.765 = 76.5%
η_total = 76.5%

CH 5

Series DC Circuits

⏱️ ~3.5 hours total 📖 Sections 5.1–5.14 🎯 Priority: High

5.1–5.2 Series Resistors & Total Resistance

⏱️ 30 min

Elements are in series when they share exactly one common point and carry the same current. For resistors in series, resistances simply add:

Total series resistanceRT = R₁ + R₂ + R₃ + ... + RN

If N equal resistors of value R are in series: RT = N × R.

EXAMPLE 5.1 — Total Series Resistance (Boylestad Ex. 5.3)

Problem: Find RT for R₁ = 4.7 kΩ, R₂ = 1 kΩ (×3, i.e. three 1 kΩ resistors), R₃ = 2.2 kΩ, all in series.

R_T = R₁ + R₃ + (N)(R₂) = 4.7 kΩ + 2.2 kΩ + (3)(1 kΩ) = 9.9 kΩ
R_T = 9.9 kΩ

5.3 Series Circuits — Current & Direction

⏱️ 30 min
Source current in a series circuitIs = E / RT
Golden rule of series circuits: The current is the same at every point. Conventional current leaves the + terminal of the source and returns to the − terminal.
EXAMPLE 5.2 — Full Series Circuit Analysis (Boylestad Fig. 5.12)

Problem: A circuit has E = 8.4 V driving three series resistors: R₁ = 10 Ω, R₂ = 30 Ω, R₃ = 100 Ω. Find RT, Is, and the voltage across each resistor.

Fig. 5.12 — Series circuit
Total resistance
R_T = 10 + 30 + 100 = 140 Ω
Current (same everywhere)
I_s = E/R_T = 8.4 V / 140 Ω = 0.06 A = 60 mA
Voltage drops (Ohm's Law on each resistor)
V₁ = I·R₁ = (0.06)(10) = 0.6 V
V₂ = I·R₂ = (0.06)(30) = 1.8 V
V₃ = I·R₃ = (0.06)(100) = 6.0 V
Check: V₁+V₂+V₃ = 0.6+1.8+6.0 = 8.4 V = E ✓

5.4 Power Distribution in a Series Circuit

⏱️ 15 min

Total power delivered by the source equals the sum of power dissipated by each resistor (conservation of energy):

Power balancePdelivered = P₁ + P₂ + P₃ + ... = E × Is

5.5–5.6 Voltage Sources in Series & Kirchhoff's Voltage Law (KVL) ⭐

⏱️ 40 min — KVL is tested in almost every exam

Kirchhoff's Voltage Law: The algebraic sum of voltage rises and drops around any closed loop is zero.

KVLΣVloop = 0   or equivalently   E = V₁ + V₂ + V₃ + ...

Procedure: pick a direction around the loop (commonly clockwise). Going from − to + across an element is a rise (+); going from + to − is a drop (−). Sum them and set equal to zero.

EXAMPLE 5.3 — Applying KVL to Find an Unknown Voltage (Boylestad Ex. 5.13)

Problem: In a series loop, E = 54 V, V₁ = 18 V, V₃ = 15 V. Find V₂, then I₂ (given R₂ = 7 Ω), then R₁ and R₃ (given I = 3 A throughout).

KVL
E = V₁ + V₂ + V₃ → V₂ = E − V₁ − V₃ = 54 − 18 − 15 = 21 V
Current
I₂ = V₂/R₂ = 21 V / 7 Ω = 3 A
Resistances
R₁ = V₁/I = 18/3 = 6 Ω    R₃ = V₃/I = 15/3 = 5 Ω
V₂ = 21 V, I = 3 A, R₁ = 6 Ω, R₃ = 5 Ω
Common mistake: Forgetting that voltage sources in series can either aid (same polarity orientation, voltages add) or oppose (opposite orientation, voltages subtract) each other. Always check polarity arrows/signs carefully before adding.

5.7 Voltage Divider Rule (VDR) ⭐⭐

⏱️ 40 min — guaranteed exam formula, master this

The VDR lets you find the voltage across any series resistor directly — without first finding the current.

Voltage Divider RuleVx = Rx × (E / RT)

Key insight: The larger the resistance, the larger the share of voltage it captures. Voltage divides in direct proportion to resistance — and this is true regardless of the actual magnitude of the resistors, only their ratio matters.

EXAMPLE 5.4 — Voltage Divider Rule (Boylestad Fig. 5.33/5.35 style)

Problem: E = 20 V across series resistors R₁ = 6 Ω, R₂ = 3 Ω, R₃ = 1 Ω. Find V₁, V₂, V₃ using VDR.

Total resistance
R_T = 6+3+1 = 10 Ω
Apply VDR to each
V₁ = R₁(E/R_T) = 6(20/10) = 12 V
V₂ = R₂(E/R_T) = 3(20/10) = 6 V
V₃ = R₃(E/R_T) = 1(20/10) = 2 V
V₁=12V, V₂=6V, V₃=2V — check: 12+6+2=20V ✓
Sanity-check trick used by the textbook: Before calculating, glance at the resistor ratios. If R₁ is 1000× bigger than R₃, expect V₁ to be ~1000× bigger than V₃. This catches arithmetic mistakes instantly on an exam.

5.9 Notation (Double-Subscript & Single-Subscript)

⏱️ 20 min
  • Vab = voltage of point a with respect to point b
  • Va = voltage of point a with respect to ground (single subscript, ground implied)
  • If Vab is negative, point a is actually at a lower potential than point b

5.10 Voltage Regulation & Internal Resistance

⏱️ 25 min

Real sources have internal resistance Rint, which causes terminal voltage to sag under load:

Terminal voltage under loadVL = E − I×Rint
Voltage Regulation (VR)VR% = [(VNL − VFL) / VFL] × 100%

where VNL = no-load voltage, VFL = full-load voltage. Lower VR% = better (stiffer) source.

EXAMPLE 5.5 — Voltage Regulation

Problem: A supply reads 12.6 V with no load and 11.8 V at full load. Find the voltage regulation.

VR% = [(12.6 − 11.8)/11.8] × 100% = (0.8/11.8) × 100% ≈ 6.78%
VR ≈ 6.78%

Parallel DC Circuits

⏱️ ~3.5 hours total 📖 Sections 6.1–6.14 🎯 Priority: High

6.2 Parallel Resistors & Total Resistance ⭐

⏱️ 40 min

Elements are in parallel when they share both end-points, so the voltage across each is identical.

General parallel resistance (any number of resistors)1/RT = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃ + ... + 1/RN
Special case — exactly two resistors (product over sum)RT = (R₁ × R₂) / (R₁ + R₂)
Special case — N equal resistorsRT = R / N
Key fact: Total parallel resistance is always smaller than the smallest individual resistor — adding more parallel paths always makes it easier for current to flow.
EXAMPLE 6.1 — Parallel Resistance via Conductance (Boylestad Ex. 6.1)

Problem: Find RT for R₁ = 3 Ω and R₂ = 6 Ω in parallel, using conductance.

Conductances
G₁ = 1/R₁ = 1/3 = 0.333 S    G₂ = 1/R₂ = 1/6 = 0.167 S
Total conductance & resistance
G_T = 0.333+0.167 = 0.5 S → R_T = 1/G_T = 1/0.5 = 2 Ω
R_T = 2 Ω  (check via product/sum: (3)(6)/(3+6) = 18/9 = 2 Ω ✓)
Speed tip for exams: For two resistors, skip conductance entirely and use product-over-sum directly: R_T = R₁R₂/(R₁+R₂). It's faster and less error-prone.

6.3 Parallel Circuits — Voltage & Current

⏱️ 30 min
Golden rule of parallel circuitsV₁ = V₂ = V₃ = ... = E (same voltage across every branch)
Source currentIs = E / RT
EXAMPLE 6.2 — Full Parallel Circuit Analysis (Boylestad Fig. 6.18)

Problem: E = 12 V supplies R₁ = 1 kΩ and R₂ = 3 kΩ in parallel. Find RT, I₁, I₂, and Is.

Fig. 6.18 — Parallel circuit
Total resistance
R_T = (1kΩ)(3kΩ)/(1kΩ+3kΩ) = 3/4 = 0.75 kΩ
Branch currents (Ohm's law — same V=12V across both)
I₁ = E/R₁ = 12V/1kΩ = 12 mA
I₂ = E/R₂ = 12V/3kΩ = 4 mA
Source current
I_s = E/R_T = 12V/0.75kΩ = 16 mA  (check: I₁+I₂ = 12+4 = 16 mA ✓)
R_T = 0.75 kΩ, I₁ = 12 mA, I₂ = 4 mA, I_s = 16 mA

6.4 Power Distribution in a Parallel Circuit

⏱️ 15 min

Same principle as series: total power = sum of branch powers = E × Is. The branch with the smallest resistance dissipates the most power (since P = V²/R and V is fixed).

6.5 Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL) ⭐

⏱️ 35 min — tested as often as KVL

Kirchhoff's Current Law: The sum of currents entering a node equals the sum of currents leaving that node (conservation of charge).

KCLΣIentering = ΣIleaving
EXAMPLE 6.3 — Applying KCL

Problem: At a node, Is = 16 mA enters, and it splits into three branches: I₁ = 6 mA, I₂ = 4 mA, and I₃ = unknown. Find I₃.

I_s = I₁ + I₂ + I₃ → 16 = 6 + 4 + I₃ → I₃ = 6 mA
I₃ = 6 mA

6.6 Current Divider Rule (CDR) ⭐⭐

⏱️ 40 min — guaranteed exam formula, master this

The CDR finds the current through any one parallel branch directly:

Current Divider Rule (general)Ix = (RT / Rx) × IT
Special case — exactly 2 branchesI₁ = R₂IT/(R₁+R₂)    I₂ = R₁IT/(R₁+R₂)
Key fact — the opposite of VDR: The smaller resistor gets the larger share of current (current takes the path of least resistance), whereas in series circuits the larger resistor gets the larger share of voltage.
EXAMPLE 6.4 — Current Divider Rule (two-branch shortcut)

Problem: IT = 16 mA splits between R₁ = 1 kΩ and R₂ = 3 kΩ in parallel. Find I₁ and I₂.

I₁ = R₂·I_T/(R₁+R₂) = (3kΩ)(16mA)/(4kΩ) = 12 mA
I₂ = R₁·I_T/(R₁+R₂) = (1kΩ)(16mA)/(4kΩ) = 4 mA
I₁ = 12 mA, I₂ = 4 mA — matches Example 6.2's direct Ohm's-law results ✓

6.7–6.8 Voltage Sources in Parallel & Open/Short Circuits

⏱️ 25 min
  • Only sources of identical voltage should be paralleled (mismatched sources cause large circulating currents).
  • Open circuit: infinite resistance, zero current through that branch.
  • Short circuit: zero resistance, all current diverts through the short, bypassing other elements.
Exam favorite: "What happens to the other branch currents if one parallel branch opens?" Answer: the remaining branches are unaffected in voltage (still = E), but total current Is decreases since RT increases.

CH 7

Series-Parallel Circuits

⏱️ ~3 hours total 📖 Sections 7.1–7.11 🎯 Priority: High

⭐ The most exam-likely circuit type

Real circuits are rarely purely series or purely parallel. This chapter combines everything from Ch. 5 & 6 into a single systematic method — this is what most multi-part exam problems look like.

7.1–7.2 Identifying Series-Parallel Relationships

⏱️ 30 min

Before doing any math, trace the circuit to identify which elements are truly in series (same current, single path) and which are in parallel (same voltage, shared nodes). Mislabeling this is the #1 cause of wrong answers in this chapter.

Two-element test: Elements are in series only if they share exactly one connection point with no other branch tapping off it. Elements are in parallel only if both their terminals connect to the same two nodes.

7.3 The Reduce-and-Return Approach (Ladder Method) ⭐⭐

⏱️ 60 min — THE core technique of this chapter, practice many problems

This is the systematic 5-step method used in nearly every solved example:

  1. Reduce: Starting from the part of the circuit farthest from the source, combine series and parallel groups step-by-step until the whole network becomes a single equivalent resistance RT.
  2. Find total current: Is = E / RT (simple Ohm's Law on the fully-reduced circuit).
  3. Return: Work back outward through your reduction steps, using the current divider rule (CDR) and voltage divider rule (VDR) to find currents/voltages at each stage.
  4. Verify: Check with KCL (currents into a node = currents out) and KVL (voltages around a loop = 0).
EXAMPLE 7.1 — Full Reduce-and-Return Solution (Boylestad Ex. 7.4 — Fig. 7.12)

Problem: E = 16.8 V feeds: R₁=9Ω ∥ R₂=6Ω (call this group A, in series with the rest), then this connects to a node b where two parallel branches exist: branch B = R₃(4Ω) in series with [R₄=6Ω ∥ R₅=3Ω], and branch C = R₆=3Ω. Find all currents.

Fig. 7.12 — Series-parallel network (block view)
Step 1 — Reduce each block
R_A = R₁∥R₂ = (9)(6)/(9+6) = 54/15 = 3.6 Ω
R₄∥R₅ = (6)(3)/(6+3) = 18/9 = 2 Ω
R_B = R₃ + (R₄∥R₅) = 4 + 2 = 6 Ω
R_C = R₆ = 3 Ω
Step 2 — Combine B ∥ C, then add A (series) for R_T
R_T = R_A + (R_B ∥ R_C) = 3.6 + (6)(3)/(6+3) = 3.6 + 2 = 5.6 Ω
Step 3 — Total (source) current
I_s = E/R_T = 16.8 V / 5.6 Ω = 3 A   (this is also I_A, since A is in series with the source)
Step 4 — Return: split current at the B‖C node using CDR
I_B = R_C·I_A/(R_C+R_B) = (3)(3)/(3+6) = 9/9 = 1 A
I_C = I_A − I_B = 3 − 1 = 2 A  (via KCL)
I_s = I_A = 3 A,   I_B = 1 A,   I_C = 2 A
Exam strategy: Always redraw the reduced circuit at each stage (like the textbook's Fig. 7.13) — a clean small sketch prevents you from losing track of which resistor combination maps to which physical part of the original circuit.

7.4 Block Diagram Approach

⏱️ 20 min

For complex networks, mentally group resistors into labeled "blocks" (A, B, C...) first, solve the simplified block-level circuit, then drill back down into each block. This is exactly the technique used in Example 7.1 above — it scales to any level of complexity.

7.6 Ladder Networks

⏱️ 25 min

A ladder network alternates series and parallel elements down a chain (resembling a ladder's rails and rungs). Solve using reduce-and-return starting from the far end (away from the source) and working back to find RT, then return with VDR/CDR to find each rung's voltage/current.

7.7 Voltage Divider Supply (Loaded vs Unloaded)

⏱️ 25 min

An unloaded voltage divider uses only resistors to set tap voltages via VDR. Once you connect a real load (RL) to a tap point, that tap's resistor is now in parallel with RL, which pulls the output voltage down from its unloaded value.

Common mistake: Forgetting to recompute the parallel combination once a load is attached. The "loaded" voltage is always ≤ the "unloaded" voltage at that tap.

7.8 Potentiometer Loading

⏱️ 15 min

Similar effect: connecting a load across part of a potentiometer's resistance changes the effective voltage division, especially severe when RL is comparable to or smaller than the potentiometer's total resistance.

7.9 Ammeter, Voltmeter & Ohmmeter Design

⏱️ 20 min
  • Ammeter: a sensitive galvanometer with a small shunt resistor in parallel to bypass most of the current
  • Voltmeter: a galvanometer with a large multiplier resistor in series to limit current and extend the voltage range
  • Ohmmeter: internal battery + galvanometer + adjustable resistor, calibrated so full-scale deflection = 0 Ω

CH 8

Methods of Analysis & Selected Topics (DC)

⏱️ ~4.5 hours total 📖 Sections 8.1–8.12 🎯 Priority: Highest

⭐ Heaviest math chapter — budget extra time

This chapter introduces systematic, general-purpose methods (branch-current, mesh, nodal analysis) that work on any circuit, even ones too complex for simple series-parallel reduction. Expect at least one full multi-part question from this chapter.

8.2 Branch-Current Analysis

⏱️ 50 min

The most fundamental (if tedious) method — define an unknown current in every branch, then solve for them all using KVL and KCL together.

Procedure:
  1. Assign a current variable and arbitrary direction to every branch
  2. Mark resistor polarities consistent with your assumed current directions
  3. Apply KVL around each independent closed loop
  4. Apply KCL at enough nodes to get one equation per unknown
  5. Solve the resulting simultaneous equations (substitution or determinants)
EXAMPLE 8.1 — Branch-Current Method (Boylestad Ex. 8.9, Fig. 8.22)

Problem: A circuit has two batteries E₁ = 2 V and E₂ = 6 V, with R₁ = 2 Ω in series with E₁, R₂ = 1 Ω in series with E₂, and R₃ = 4 Ω as a shared middle branch joining both loops. Find I₁, I₂, I₃.

Fig. 8.22 — Two-loop network, three branches
KVL — Loop 1 (clockwise)
+2 V − (2Ω)I₁ − (4Ω)I₃ = 0
KVL — Loop 2 (clockwise)
(4Ω)I₃ + (1Ω)I₂ − 6 V = 0
KCL at the shared node
I₁ + I₂ = I₃
Solving the 3×3 system (via determinants)
I₁ = −1 A    I₂ = 2 A    I₃ = 1 A
I₁ = −1 A (actual direction opposite to assumed), I₂ = 2 A, I₃ = 1 A
Reminder: A negative result doesn't mean an error — it means the actual current flows opposite to the direction you assumed. Keep the magnitude and flip the arrow.

8.3–8.4 Determinants Method (Cramer's Rule)

⏱️ 35 min

For a 2×2 system a₁x + b₁y = c₁, a₂x + b₂y = c₂:

2×2 Determinant solutionx = (c₁b₂ − c₂b₁)/(a₁b₂ − a₂b₁)    y = (a₁c₂ − a₂c₁)/(a₁b₂ − a₂b₁)

For 3×3 systems, expand along any row/column using cofactors, or use a calculator/Mathcad as the textbook demonstrates. On a timed exam, a scientific calculator with matrix/determinant functions is a huge time-saver — check if yours is allowed.

8.6–8.7 Mesh (Loop) Analysis — General Approach ⭐⭐

⏱️ 70 min — the single highest-yield topic in the course

Mesh analysis is far more efficient than branch-current for circuits with many elements. Instead of one variable per branch, you assign one variable per independent loop (mesh).

Procedure:
  1. Assign a clockwise mesh current to each independent (window) loop
  2. Indicate resistor voltage polarities based on the assumed mesh current direction
  3. Apply KVL around each mesh — for resistors shared between two meshes, include both mesh currents (subtracting the adjacent mesh's current since it flows opposite through the shared element)
  4. Solve the resulting system of equations
EXAMPLE 8.2 — Mesh Analysis, Two Independent Loops (Boylestad Ex. 8.12, Fig. 8.31)

Problem: Loop 1 has E₁ = 5 V and R₁ = 1 Ω; loop 2 has E₂ = 10 V and R₃ = 2 Ω; the two loops share R₂ = 6 Ω. Find both mesh currents.

Fig. 8.31 — Two-mesh network
Loop 1 KVL (clockwise)
+5 V − (1Ω)I₁ − (6Ω)(I₁ − I₂) = 0  →  −7I₁ + 6I₂ = −5
Loop 2 KVL (clockwise)
−(6Ω)(I₂ − I₁) − (2Ω)I₂ + 10 V = 0  →  +6I₁ − 8I₂ = −10
Solving simultaneously
I₁ = 1 A    I₂ = 2 A
Current through the shared 6 Ω resistor
I_R2 = I₂ − I₁ = 2 − 1 = 1 A (in the direction of I₂)
I₁ = 1 A, I₂ = 2 A, I through shared resistor = 1 A
Format/shortcut approach: If every source is a voltage source and every mesh current is drawn clockwise, you can write the matrix directly: diagonal terms = sum of resistances in that loop; off-diagonal terms = negative of the shared resistance between loops; right-hand side = net voltage source in that loop (rises positive). This avoids re-deriving KVL by inspection every time.

8.8 Mesh Analysis — Current Sources & Supermesh

⏱️ 30 min

When a current source sits on the boundary between two meshes (and has no resistance in parallel to convert it), form a supermesh: mentally remove the current source, apply KVL around the resulting combined outer loop, then add a KCL equation relating the mesh currents to the known source current.

8.9 Nodal Analysis — General Approach ⭐⭐

⏱️ 70 min — equally high-yield, often paired with mesh analysis on exams

Nodal analysis solves for node voltages (relative to a chosen reference/ground node) instead of currents.

Procedure:
  1. Count the nodes; pick one as the reference (0 V) — for N nodes you need (N−1) equations
  2. Label the unknown node voltages V₁, V₂, ...
  3. At each non-reference node, apply KCL: assume all unknown currents leave the node, and express each in terms of node voltages divided by resistance (Ohm's law: I = V/R)
  4. Solve the resulting simultaneous equations for the node voltages
EXAMPLE 8.3 — Nodal Analysis (Boylestad Ex. 8.21, Fig. 8.54)

Problem: A 4 A current source feeds node V₁ (with R₁ = 2 Ω to ground), connected to node V₂ (with R₂ = 6 Ω to ground) through R₃ = 12 Ω, and a 2 A current source pulls current out of node V₂. Find V₁ and V₂.

Fig. 8.54 — Two-node network
KCL at node V₁
4 A = V₁/R₁ + (V₁−V₂)/R₃ = V₁/2 + (V₁−V₂)/12
KCL at node V₂
(V₂−V₁)/R₃ + V₂/R₂ + 2 A = 0  →  (V₂−V₁)/12 + V₂/6 = −2 A
Rearranged into standard form & solved
7V₁ − V₂ = 48     −V₁ + 3V₂ = −24
Result (via determinants)
V₁ = +6 V    V₂ = −6 V
V₁ = 6 V, V₂ = −6 V. Then I_R3 = (V₁−V₂)/12 = 12/12 = 1 A, flowing from node 1 to node 2
Common mistake: Forgetting that ALL unknown currents are assumed to leave the node when writing the KCL equation — stay consistent across all nodes, regardless of what assumption you used for other nodes (each node's equation is independent).

8.10 Bridge Networks

⏱️ 25 min
Bridge network — balance condition

Balanced when R₁/R₃ = R₂/R₄ — the galvanometer (G) reads zero, no matter what voltage drives the bridge.

A bridge network cannot be reduced by simple series-parallel rules. It is balanced (I = 0, V = 0 across the bridge/galvanometer arm) when:

Bridge balance conditionR₁ / R₃ = R₂ / R₄

Unbalanced bridges require mesh or nodal analysis to solve fully.

8.12 Y-Δ (Wye-Delta) and Δ-Y Conversions

⏱️ 30 min

Some resistor networks (especially bridges) are neither purely series nor parallel until you convert between Wye (Y) and Delta (Δ) configurations.

Δ → Y (each Y resistor)
R_Y = (product of 2 adjacent Δ resistors) / (sum of all 3 Δ resistors)
Y → Δ (each Δ resistor)
R_Δ = (sum of all Y cross-products) / (opposite Y resistor)
Special case worth memorizing: If all three Δ resistors equal R, then each Y resistor = R/3. Conversely, if all three Y resistors equal R, each Δ resistor = 3R.

CH 9

Network Theorems

⏱️ ~4 hours total 📖 Sections 9.1–9.9 🎯 Priority: Highest

⭐ Almost always a full exam question

These theorems let you analyze just the part of a circuit you care about (e.g., one load resistor) without re-solving the whole network. Thévenin and Superposition especially are near-universal exam topics.

9.2 Superposition Theorem ⭐

⏱️ 50 min

In a linear circuit with multiple independent sources, the total response (current or voltage) at any point equals the sum of the responses caused by each source acting alone, with all other sources set to zero (voltage sources → short circuit; current sources → open circuit).

Procedure:
  1. Pick one source; "zero" all others (short voltage sources, open current sources)
  2. Solve the simplified circuit for the variable of interest
  3. Repeat for each remaining source individually
  4. Algebraically add all the individual contributions (respecting direction/polarity)
EXAMPLE 9.1 — Superposition with 3 Sources (Boylestad Ex. 9.5)

Problem: Find the current through R₁ = 2 Ω in a circuit with three sources: E₁ = 12 V and E₂ = 6 V (both in series with R₁=2Ω and R₂=4Ω in the loop), and a 3 A current source, with R₂ = 4 Ω.

Boylestad Ex. 9.5 — Two voltage sources + one current source
Effect of E₁ = 12 V alone (short E₂ and open the current source)
I'₁ = E₁/(R₁+R₂) = 12V/(2Ω+4Ω) = 12/6 = 2 A
Effect of E₂ = 6 V alone
I''₁ = E₂/(R₁+R₂) = 6V/6Ω = 1 A
Effect of the 3 A current source alone (use current divider rule)
I'''₁ = R₂·I/(R₁+R₂) = (4Ω)(3A)/6Ω = 12/6 = 2 A
Superpose — add algebraically, respecting each contribution's direction
I₁ = I''₁ + I'''₁ − I'₁ = 1 A + 2 A − 2 A = 1 A  (actual result from the book)
I₁ = 1 A (total, combining all three sources)
Common mistake: Superposition applies only to voltage and current (linear quantities). You cannot superpose power directly — power depends on the square of current/voltage, so you must find total current/voltage first, then compute power once at the end.

9.3 Thévenin's Theorem ⭐⭐

⏱️ 70 min — the single most important theorem in the course

Any two-terminal linear network can be replaced by a single equivalent voltage source ETh in series with a single equivalent resistance RTh.

Procedure:
  1. Remove the load resistor RL; label the two open terminals a and b
  2. Redraw the remaining network
  3. Find RTh: zero all sources (short voltage sources, open current sources), then find the resistance looking into terminals a-b
  4. Find ETh: restore all sources, then find the open-circuit voltage between a and b
  5. Draw the Thévenin equivalent (ETh in series with RTh), reconnect RL, and solve with simple Ohm's Law
EXAMPLE 9.2 — Full Thévenin Procedure (Boylestad Ex. 9.6, Fig. 9.26)

Problem: A network has E = 9 V in series with R₁ = 3 Ω, feeding a node where R₂ = 6 Ω is in parallel with the load RL. Find the Thévenin equivalent, then IL for RL = 2 Ω, 10 Ω, and 100 Ω.

Fig. 9.26 — Original network → Thévenin equivalent
Step 3 — R_Th (short the source E)
R_Th = R₁ ∥ R₂ = (3Ω)(6Ω)/(3Ω+6Ω) = 18/9 = 2 Ω
Step 4 — E_Th (restore source, use VDR for the open-circuit voltage across R₂)
E_Th = R₂·E/(R₁+R₂) = (6Ω)(9V)/9Ω = 54/9 = 6 V
Step 5 — Apply to each load value
R_L=2Ω:  I_L = E_Th/(R_Th+R_L) = 6/(2+2) = 1.5 A
R_L=10Ω: I_L = 6/(2+10) = 0.5 A
R_L=100Ω: I_L = 6/(2+100) = 0.06 A
R_Th = 2 Ω, E_Th = 6 V — once found, ANY load value takes one line of arithmetic
Why this matters: Without Thévenin's theorem, changing RL would require re-analyzing the entire original circuit from scratch each time. With it, you do the hard work once and then plug in any RL instantly — exactly why exams love testing this.

9.4 Norton's Theorem

⏱️ 35 min

The current-source counterpart to Thévenin: any two-terminal linear network can be replaced by a single current source IN in parallel with a single resistance RN.

Key relationship — Norton ↔ ThéveninRN = RTh    IN = ETh/RTh    ETh = IN×RN

Find RN exactly like RTh (zero the sources, look into the terminals). Find IN as the short-circuit current between the two open terminals (with all sources active).

EXAMPLE 9.3 — Norton's Theorem, Same Circuit as Example 9.2 (Boylestad Ex. 9.11)

Problem: Using the identical network (E=9V, R₁=3Ω, R₂=6Ω), find the Norton equivalent.

Norton equivalent of the same network
R_N (identical method to R_Th)
R_N = R₁ ∥ R₂ = (3)(6)/9 = 2 Ω
I_N — short terminals a-b; this shorts out R₂, so all source current flows through R₁ only
I_N = E/R₁ = 9V/3Ω = 3 A
R_N = 2 Ω, I_N = 3 A — check: E_Th = I_N × R_N = 3A × 2Ω = 6 V ✓ matches Example 9.2's Thévenin result exactly

9.5 Maximum Power Transfer Theorem ⭐

⏱️ 35 min

Maximum power is delivered to a load when the load resistance equals the Thévenin resistance of the source network feeding it.

Maximum power transfer conditionRL = RTh
Maximum power deliveredPL,max = ETh² / (4×RTh)
EXAMPLE 9.4 — Maximum Power Transfer (Boylestad Fig. 9.79, Table 9.1)

Problem: A Thévenin equivalent has ETh = 60 V, RTh = 9 Ω. Find RL for maximum power transfer, and the value of that maximum power.

Fig. 9.79 — Maximum power transfer condition
Matching condition
R_L = R_Th = 9 Ω
Maximum power
P_max = E_Th²/(4R_Th) = (60)²/(4×9) = 3600/36 = 100 W
Verify via I and V at the matched point
I_L = E_Th/(2R_Th) = 60/18 = 3.33 A (half of I_max=6.6A)  |  V_L = E_Th/2 = 30 V (half of E_Th)
R_L = 9 Ω for P_max = 100 W
Conceptual fact worth memorizing: At maximum power transfer, efficiency is only 50% — exactly half the power delivered by the source is lost in RTh itself. Maximum power ≠ maximum efficiency.

9.6 Millman's Theorem

⏱️ 25 min

A fast method to combine multiple parallel voltage sources (each with their own series resistance) into one equivalent source.

Millman's TheoremEeq = (E₁G₁ + E₂G₂ + ...) / (G₁ + G₂ + ...)    where G = 1/R
Equivalent resistanceReq = 1 / (G₁ + G₂ + ...)

9.7–9.8 Substitution & Reciprocity Theorems

⏱️ 20 min — lighter conceptual content
  • Substitution Theorem: If you know the voltage across (or current through) any branch, you can replace that branch with any combination of elements that produces the same V/I, without affecting the rest of the circuit.
  • Reciprocity Theorem: In a single-source linear circuit, swapping the positions of the source and an ammeter (response) gives the same current reading — the source and response points are interchangeable.

⚠️ Top 10 mistakes that lose exam marks

  1. Mixing up VDR (larger R → more voltage) and CDR (smaller R → more current)
  2. Forgetting units or mixing kΩ/Ω/MΩ within the same calculation
  3. Not double-checking with KVL/KCL after solving — always verify your answer sums correctly
  4. Assuming a negative mesh/branch current is wrong — it just means the actual direction is opposite
  5. Adding powers of ten without first matching the exponents
  6. Forgetting that superposition applies to V and I, NOT power directly
  7. Using the general parallel formula when the 2-resistor product-over-sum shortcut would be faster
  8. Forgetting to zero out ALL other sources (not just one) when finding R_Th
  9. Mixing up R_Th (open-circuit based) with R_N — remember R_N = R_Th always
  10. Rushing the circuit-tracing step in Ch 7 — misidentifying series vs. parallel dooms the entire problem
